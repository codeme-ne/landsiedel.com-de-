NLP for teachers

Strategies for the classroom and self-management

Use the tips ...



NLP for teachers – your e-book for 0€

In this e-book you will find out ...

  • ... how you can build a good connection to your students via Repeat
  • ... how you recognize and optimally support different types of learning
  • ... what you can do in stressful situations to be relaxed and powerful again

Preface

Do you know that? You chose the teacher's profession out of conviction and started with a lot of commitment. Maybe it's still your dream job, maybe you have experienced one or the other frustration in everyday work.

Large and heterogeneous classes, increasingly "difficult" pupils, inclusion and migration – these are just a few topics that present teachers today with challenges. Technical, educational and didactical they are usually excellently trained. However, in everyday school life, good strategies for dealing with class dynamics, individual discussions and own stress management are also in demand.

In this e-book I have therefore put together a few tried and tested NLP tools especially for teachers. So that learning together can be more fun – and your dream job remains a dream job.

NLP at school – a word in advance

Again and again the remark emerges that: NLP It is true that teachers have a great influence on their pupils. In the first years of school, they are usually a reference person and a role model; in higher years, they have the power to influence the further career through grades. In other words, teachers "manipulate – that is, shape – their pupils anyway. That is not avoidable. Be it by their teaching style, by praise and admonition, by the mood in the classroom. Sometimes even an unconsidered positive or negative remark is enough to change the motivation of a student.

And let's say that some teachers are more likely to suffer from the situation of apparently not being able to influence their students, e.g. to ensure calmness and concentration.

What if you could use your skills to influence the students in a positive way? For example, away from school displeasure and towards more motivation? By building trust and optimally supporting them in their learning?

1. Strategies in the classroom

The good connection to the students

In the NLP, we call the good link between people "Repeat. Rapport creates a trusting basis and is an important prerequisite for a positive learning atmosphere.

Probably there are a lot of students, colleagues and parents with whom you get along right away. You speak the same language and the chemistry is right from the start. You have relationship with each other. Of course there are people with whom it doesn't go so well. What do you do then? Usually these people are just different from us – and we don't find them likeable right away.

You can build up rapport to another person by aligning yourself to them. Reflect discreetly their posture, i.e. stand or sit down as they do. Take over their facial expressions. Talk at a similar speaking speed as they do, use the same terms that they use. In the NLP, we call this "PacenIt's not about aftertaste. It's about the purposeful construction of a good communication basis. You get involved with the other. It's like talking in an untrusted foreign language and serving to get on the same radio frequency. Once it's reached, you have a repeat.

What does Rapport do to the students?

Rapport makes a student feel comfortable talking to you, making it easier to access his resources, and in a resourceful state, he becomes more receptive to the learning material.

Build up rapport to the whole class

Of course, you can't reflect the posture of each student at the same time. Here NLP offers other possibilities:

Place yourself where you usually present and let your gaze wander from student to student. You can do this relatively quickly, e.g. when you welcome the class at the beginning of the class. Try to catch everyone with your gaze once. You can smile, nod the students or maybe wink at them with your eyes. Nonverbal gives you the message that you are present and that you have perceived each one.

In addition, you can say to yourself in thoughts about the following: "Beautiful that you are there" or "You are great people." This inner attitude is felt outwardly.

Our Attitude Creates Reality

In a well-known experiment (Pygmalion effect), teachers were suggested that certain pupils were particularly talented and gifted. Teachers were prepared to do so and gave them more attention. For example, they gave them more time for their answers and encouraged them more overall. These pupils actually showed higher achievements later on. However, they had been randomly selected and named by lot method, regardless of their actual potential. The Pygmalion effect clearly shows how very positive attitudes towards the students can be. Conversely, some students are very quickly classified as difficult, sometimes even because of their first name.

So your assumptions and attitudes influence your students. Presumably they do their best. Helpful attitudes are e.g. "I know you can do it!" or "You have a great potential." Maybe you just want to try it out?

Anchor in the classroom

Anchor allow us to retrieve desired states (e.g. joy, calmness, attention) at the touch of a button. When anchoring, an external, neutral stimulus is coupled with a certain emotion, so that the same emotion occurs automatically when the stimulus is triggered again.

Anchors can arise unintentionally, for example when a student accidentally looks at a certain image on the wall when he is afraid of a bad note. The sight of the picture alone can then bring him back to a bad state at any time without being aware of why.

Anchors can be used in the classroom to easily evoke certain desired states of the class. I would like to introduce you here "Floor anchors", selected places in the room that you can link to different states. Once you have established them well, you can automatically provide for rest and attention, for example, without using time again each time.

Homework as anchor

Always put yourself in the same place when you are trying to do your homework. This can be, for example, next to a board where the tasks are recorded. It is important to always use the same place. Combine it with a specific way of speaking, e.g. especially objectively or clearly. If you regularly announce the tasks at this place, the students automatically pay more attention to you. Even if you announce important learning material for an exam in between, use the homework space.

Presentation site

Use the place where you use fabric as your power place. You can anchor this place positively and powerfully if you are undisturbed alone in the classroom:

Think, for example, of a situation in which you have experienced yourself particularly competently or strongly. Imagine that you would just experience this situation and the corresponding feeling again. If you feel this feeling particularly intense, then stand on your presentation site. Enjoy the feeling of competence or strength while you are standing there.

If you want, you can anchor another positive feeling in the same way with this place.

As soon as you enter your power field in your class, you will feel the effect. And it will also transfer to your class.

Questionnaires

You can also look for a place in the room where, for example, you have to answer questions from the students or admonish the class. In this case, get away from your powerful presentation space and keep it free from unpleasant emotions.

For the students it will be unconsciously noticeable that you speak particularly powerfully when you explain new stuff, that you announce the tasks in another place, etc.

Different types of learning

We humans absorb information through our five senses and encode it in our brain. We usually have a preferred Sensual channelthat we learn particularly well about.

Visual students like to see well-designed table pictures, graphics and documents, auditory listen to the information and want to get the material explained. And kinesthetic students (feeling types) literally understand the most when they can do something and try it out.

Design your lessons so that each of these main channels is covered. Visualize, tell and let the students do something. This allows you to optimally prepare and convey the learning material. In addition, you strengthen the relationship to the class.

Of course, we can also record information on the other channels. A few students, however, are only visually, only auditory or only kinesthetic accessible. They only take up the information delayed because they have to translate the mediated material inside their channel. That's about as if you were listening to a lecture in a foreign language that you don't master 100%. Therefore, it may be that you are already on the topic until the important information has arrived at these "translators". This can lead to unrest and learning displeasure.

If you notice these students, you can find out what their main perception channel is. You can hear that out of the terms they use, among other things.

Visual students use visual terms such as "see, black-and-white, show me that, I can't see it, it's very blurry for me, I look black-and-white, etc. You hear in auditory schoolgirls "that sounds good, that's not right, that's annoying, that's not really ticking. And kinesthetic students express themselves like this: "I don't, we can finally get started, that's weighing on me, I understand that, I have the impression that...

In the future, therefore, try to use the mode of these students more often when teaching. Also, choose terms from all sensory channels as possible. This makes your class look better. They listen more. And you bring them all into the boat.

What else you can do for a good class climate

In addition, you create a good learning atmosphere by making the classroom visually appealing. Make sure that a certain order is adhered to. This visually ensures well-being.

Sometimes a loud ticking watch can lead to a lack of concentration. If possible, use curtains or lamella blinds in front of the windows. They absorb loud noises and provide better acoustics.

And some learn easier if they can bring a seat cushion and sit comfortably. Or can move in between times.

3. Self- and stress management

As a teacher or teacher, you are a leader. You lead a group of people, motivate and promote them, and of course you also demand performance from them. I would like to give you a short but effective method of State Managements show with which you can quickly improve your charisma and give nonverbal strength.

Stand upright for 2-3 minutes with arms stretched upwards, slightly stretched out. This power pose makes you feel more powerful and competent quickly. You provide energy and leadership skills. It also works if you feel sluggish and unmotivated before.

Background: Amy Cuddy left students in this power pose for a few minutes in an experiment. Before and after, she tested the cortisol and testosterone levels in the saliva of the subjects. In the students who had taken a power pose, the cortisol value (our stress hormone) decreased and the testosterone value (for enforcement and energy) increased.

A second group of students then watched video recordings of the people who had previously taken the power attitude. They were trusted with more leadership skills than the people in a comparison group who had not used power pose.

You can increase the effect by smiling during your power pose.

Use this exercise also in stressful situations, e.g. before a difficult parent or student talk. By the way: You do this exercise, if you are unobserved, in the classroom or at another retreat. If you feel that your whole class might need a little more energy, then let your students also put it like that. Then let them say the following sentence: "Oh, what's wrong with me!" Since this can't be said congruently, most people probably have to laugh and you quickly changed the mood in the classroom to a positive one.

When it gets particularly violent

There are also times for experienced educators in which they are packed with stage fright. Perhaps a visit to the school council or a training course in the referendariat is on the agenda. Perhaps the class is especially difficult.

If you come into such a situation, experience a blackout or fear, you can quickly relax with the following method:

Take a ball (or an apple, an orange) and throw it from one hand to the other. Watch it as it flies from one side to the other. You won't be able to hold onto your cloudy thoughts while you do it. Reason: the eye movements that you trigger with it will immediately get you out of the blocked state.

Workplace at home

If you take home class work, make sure you only have it in a certain place. Keep your bedroom or the area where you normally relax and shut down free of school records.

The sight of the booklets and work would otherwise constantly remind you that you still have something to correct. If you are currently under stress with some students, this anchor can transfer to your apartment. Make sure that your relaxation rooms remain free.


This was a small section of the possibilities NLP teachers can offer. If you try them out, you can probably experience one or the other improvement soon.

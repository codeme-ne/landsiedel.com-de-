Perception & Perception Filter

Perception

We can only use information in our communication that we have received. This can only be done with open senses. If our perception is blocked, our communication cannot flow.

Four simple facts about our perception

(taken from an introductory lecture in psychological physiology)


  1. We do not perceive reality as it is. (Transformation in the sense organs)
  2. Only a small part of reality is perceptible to us (X-rays, infrared light, etc. - animals can perceive this)
  3. Only those who reach a certain degree of strength (different perception of pain) become aware of the processes for which we have sensory organs.
  4. Those processes that we become aware of become only imprecisely aware of in their diversity. (Experiment: Difference Thresholds of Weber)

How Our Perception Works

(taken from "The Reality of the NLP" by Alexa Mohl)

In school, we learned that the eye works like a camera. The rays of light that emanate from the objects of our environment create sharp images of the objects on the retina in the background of our eyes. This leads to the conclusion that we all have the same, namely the reality that surrounds us.

This view of the process of seeing, of visual perception, is a mistake. Seeing does not work like a camera. The reality surrounding us does not produce images in our eyes that are transmitted to the brain. One cannot therefore draw the conclusion that we all have the same before our eyes and thus share the reality surrounding us.

The idea that something isn't right could have already come to school. I remember that I asked then what could be the reason why we see things upright. If seeing worked like a camera, the pictures we see would have to be upside down. And I remember the teacher's lapidary answer: "The optic nerve turns it around!"

What we learned at school can no longer be reconciled with the results of recent neurophysiological research. Seeing doesn't work like a camera. It's not that from the reality surrounding us, images on the retina arise, which would be forwarded to the brain:

What we take pictures of reaches our eyes as physical waves. In the cells of the retina, irritated by these light waves, chemical processes take place that enter the central nervous system in the form of electrical impulses.Our brain processes these impulses into inner images.Like seeing, the other processes of perception also take place: environmental influences irritate our sensory organs, which stimulate our brain to activity.This unexplored activity of our brain produces this and determines what we call our world, images that we see, sounds and sounds that we hear, surfaces of the things we feel, and what we smell and taste.

Perception filter

We perceive the world through our five senses: we see, hear, feel, smell and taste and absorb so much information. We also represent our sensuous perception linguistically. We use expressions that refer to the visual, auditory or kinesthetic In this case, the system of the senses must be directed to the individual.

Neurological filters (limitations of sensory perceptions by nerve cells):

There are also many physically measurable phenomena that we cannot perceive due to our neurology, such as certain sound frequencies and light waves, which are of great importance to other living beings (e.g. dogs, bats, tomatoes). This means that our sensory perception is subject to physiological restrictions. Certain information is not even included here.

Cultural and social filters (restrictions of perceptions, through education and the society in which the individual lives):

In addition, our perception is also shaped by cultural and social patterns. For example, the five senses of an Australian native certainly provide very different information than that of a New Yorker. How many differences and corresponding terms for snow can we find? Three, four, five? Eskimos know and name over 20 different varieties of snow! In Congo it is recognized to perceive ghosts and ghosts - not in our society. In Congo, spirits are reality, in our society they are "phantoms".

I.e.: Even with the same Neurology The perception of the world can be very different depending on the requirements and traditions of the co-world.

Individual filters (restrictions of your perceptions that we make through our personal experiences):

Similar to the cultural ones, the individual filters also work. Due to personal experiences, we prioritize certain categories of information, while we neglect others rather or perhaps don't take notice of them at all. Our ability to filter out undesirable things in the so-called "party effect" is quite clear. It's our ability to hear a single noise from a noise setting just because we're curious about its contents.

Another example: While one can assign different motorcycle sounds to individual brands and enjoy these sounds because he is a motorcycle fan, another identifies exactly the same acoustic phenomenon as noise nuisance and can't even identify any differences "in the noise". Other statements such as "Never Help Me Someone" or "All Admir Me" illustrate another variant of selective perception.





The basic processing processes

Generalization, tilting and distortion

Above, we have seen which framework conditions influence our perception, which factors influence the design of our "Map of the World". By means of our language, we are also able to convey our perception to other people. Our way of expression not only reflects our world view, but also largely the processes by which we have created our model of the world.

They are essentially: three design processeswith which we process our experience and then represent linguistically: Generalize (generalize), Tilts and Distortion These processes allow us, on the one hand, to survive, to grow, to learn, to understand and to experience the wealth the world has to offer. On the other hand, these processes can lead to problems, since we mistakenly equate our own selective perception of reality with the outer world.

By generalizing we learn to exist in the world: For example, once we have burned ourselves on an iron as a child, we generalize these experiences to all objects that are hot and look similar. We do not have to repeat the same experience with another iron, because we have already learned the generalization of this experience.

An example of limiting generalization is, for example, a typical dog phobia. The experience of being bitten by a dog has been transferred to all existing dogs, the result being a significant impairment of everyday freedom of movement and general well-being.

The ability to use Tilgen allows us to record only the information that is important to us. Otherwise, we would be flooded with a flood of stimuli that our consciousness could not process at all. Thus, someone can read a book while people are talking about it or the TV is running.

However, if we delete or ignore any useful experience, such as in the lawsuit "I don't get any recognition", repayments will have a restrictive effect. In this statement, not only the experiences in which the person was recognized have disappeared, but also the actors of this non-recognition and what is not recognized by them, etc.

The third process with which we create our perception is distorting and fantasizing. We can use these processes to reshape and reshape our experiences as we wish. On the basis of this ability, we are able to transform dreams into reality, to paint images or to write novels.

Disadvantages of distortion can be seen in statements such as: "I regret my decision." Here, linguistically, a process - that of decision-making - is solidified into an immutable event. Thus, the speaker takes control of ongoing processes by redefining them into a fact that cannot be changed.

Graves Model (often also Spiral Dynamics)

The Graves model is often also called Spiral Dynamics. Don Beck, a student of Clare Graves, has awarded the new term. The term has become naturalised in many areas, especially as it is Ken Wilber often used.

Maslow's need pyramid

Abraham Maslow's need pyramid was the most famous model for the ontogenetic development of a human being in the 1930s. However, his theory was unfounded and gave Clare Graves - a contemporary Maslows - occasion to criticism.

In his opinion, Maslow's view of the self-updating person was too inflexible. He did not accept the endpoint of the Maslow pyramid as the endpoint of development, because Graves considered that certain value systems (=means) have developed in response to certain human conditions of existence. Such memes are, on the one hand, psychological attempts to react appropriately to certain historical conditions of existence and, on the other hand, these counter-movements to the respective prevailing system of values (antithetics).

Graves Levels

1. Person Clare W. Graves

Clare W. Graves (1914-1986) was American psychologist at Union College in New York and with his Meme model the Vordenker der Spiral Dynamics. The impulse for the development of this model was the central issue of his students: Why are people different? Why do some change? Why do some understand such completely different things as the highest human development goal of self-realization?

Grave's answer was: "In short, I say that the psychology of mature human beings is an evolving, oscillating, spiral-shaped process, emerging from previous stages, characterized by the subordination of older behavioral systems with lower rank, which progresses to newer higher systems above them when the existential problems of mankind change."

This means that human development is continuous and never finished. It changes with the change in human living conditions and thus creates new systems in which the old systems are integrated. When a new system or a new stage develops, we also change our psychology and our habits to adapt to these new conditions. The most diverse forms of life in this potentially open value system are possible. There is no final stage that everyone has to reach. The individual, companies or even entire societies can only react positively to management principles, motivational incentives, educational principles and permitted or ethical ideas that correspond to the current stage of this human existence!

Graves Value Model:

The emergent, cyclic double helix model of mature, adult biopsychosocial behavior

Short description

Each class of problems or Challenges are solved by creating a value system. Until now, mankind has produced eight different value systems: such value systems are mechanisms that the psyche has developed in order to be able to deal appropriately with certain environmental situations. If the problems and challenges facing a person or society change, then their value system also changes. Value systems are therefore appropriate if they enable a congruent problem solution behavior. However, they must not be compared independently of the context.

emergent = arising
Value systems are created by changing the environment again and again

Cyclic double helix

Each value system is created at the intersection of two forces, namely the problem and the respective problem solution approach.

A value system reflects how people think, i.e. their values and belief structures. However, they must not be confused with the subject or the respective content of thinking. Value systems are rather a scheme or a way of thinking. Frequently, value systems that are diametrically opposed in terms of content are, e.g. a fundamentalist religion and the so-called "atheistic communism", merely forms one and the same system of values.

The Graves model has an enormous explanation and prediction value and is applicable to all aspects of biological, psychological and sociological behavior. It explains both individual behavior and international political interactions: different thinking about religion, the sense of human existence, economics, politics, family, education, health and leisure, as well as different patterns of goals, ethics, motivation, lifestyle and organization. The entering or leaving of the respective system is triggered by forces from the environment in which the respective system exists. The depth structures of the Graves model however correspond to types of thinking, not types of people. These patterns form biopsychosocial systems and are neither a typology of people nor discrete evolutions of people. They reflect systems in the repertoire of human behavior, which is why every person has certain mental patterns that can be activated by different contexts and situations.

The properties of the planes

The emergent cyclic double helix model for the biopsychosocial development of adults postulates eight levels:

  • Beige: reactive, autistic, archaic and instinctive; the survival and survival struggle are the main focus
  • Violet animistic, tribalistic, safety-oriented. The person sacrifices their own wishes for the tribe, the chief or the spirits
  • Red egocentric, self-oriented, hedonistic, magical. The self wants to express itself here and now and does not care for others
  • Blue absoluteistic, compliant, religious. The self sacrifices for later wages (in heaven, etc.)
  • Orange erfolgsorientiert, materialistisch, objektivistisch. Das Selbst kalkuliert die eigenen Interessen, um nicht in Konflikt mit anderen zu geraten
  • Green Personalistic, group-oriented, humanistic. The self is ready to sacrifice itself for others
  • Yellow Systemic, existential, functional. The self is interested in the current self-expression, but not at the expense of others
  • Turquoise experience-oriented, global thinking, synergistic. The self is now ready to sacrifice itself so that life can continue.

The problems of the levels

Each level of the Graves model represents a solution strategy and a response to a class of problems. Therefore, each level can be a connection between a group of problems or Challenges that people think about to cope with the problems.

The Memes in the model of Spiral Dynamics

Introduction

The students of Clare Graves have expanded their model and created the Spiral Dynamics - one of the most precise models of cultural development. But Spiral Dynamics does not have a one-dimensional perspective and does not consider people with a cold analytical distance, but provides a clear, deep insight into the flowing patterns of human psyche. Perceptions and values are often unconsciously guided by our decisions or form our identity.

Personal experiences are placed in a historical context of our human development, which leads to a liberating objectivity. This development is part of every human being and extends from the most primitive survival instinct (beige) to spiritual aspirations (turquoise). Spiral Dynamics is designed as a spiral, as it is an expression of natural and cosmic forces that can be detected in all: The spiral shape is found in our DNA, in our galaxies, etc...
The spiral with its upwardly expanding structure best describes the evolution of human consciousness as it corresponds to the unfolding way of thinking and the steadily growing complexity. The shift to the next higher level of human development is triggered by crises and conflicts, but the spiral will never reach a final stage, but will continuously and dynamically expand upwards. Application of Graves Value Model

1. In the clarification of conflicts (quoted according to O.p.a.l.)

As a mixture of the steps blue, orange and green can normally be found in large companies, conflicts are pre-programmed.

Example:
The "oranges" reject the "blues" because they block changes from their point of view and they reject the "greens" because they always want to integrate all opinions and therefore discuss endlessly. The "greens" do not like the heartless, geld "oranges" and distrust the "blues" with their hierarchies and defined structures.

Let's fill the example with a little more life:

Personnel departments are often "green" and are committed to the welfare of employees (e.g. by personnel development, employee incentives). Management is often "orange". For them, the performance of the employees is at first place. For this reason, some managers consider staff development as a waste of time. The "greens" evaluate the "oranges" and the "oranges" do not understand the "greens".

The problem is that every person and every company considers its own level to be the only right one and attacks the others. At our current level, we also evaluate our old stages of development that we have already left behind. We have evolved and now feel ourselves raised by people of the lower levels. We cannot understand the thinking of people of the upper stages of development.
Only from the stage yellow (7 in the Graves model) we have an integral view. From this level we value people at all levels.

Two. When used for employee management

2.1 Problematik in Unternehmen

A person, a company or even an entire nation can only react positively to the management principles, motivation incentives, educational principles and ethical values that correspond to their own development stage.
This means that the individual development stages require different leadership and communication styles as well as procedures. For example, you can motivate "Blue" employees with a title or status symbol. Meanwhile, "orange" employees can be impressed with a performance-oriented premium. These incentives would be ineffective for "green" employees. Here teamwork is more promising in a "valuable" project.

However, our development stage is not static, but it changes. Every person/system develops upwards in the long term with increasing complexity of the environment. If we have adopted a new stage of development, we will change our view of the world, our way of thinking and habits of living to adapt to the new conditions. In future, we will be carrying out the ongoing stages of development. This is the same for a developing system.

However, a person/system can fall back to a lower level in crises. For example, in the case of recession and unemployment, social thinking (level green) can first go back and hard competition (level orange or red) can arise. If we have successfully mastered the crisis, we take the place on our "old" stage of development relatively quickly.

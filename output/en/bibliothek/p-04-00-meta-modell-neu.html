The meta-model of the language

Be aware of the magic of your language. It is not only the basis for clear understanding, but also for rapid and targeted change. Feel free to do this NLP technique one of the leading language tools of our time. Become a master of spoken language.

What is the meta model?

Misunderstandings in communication happen day by day. They are part of daily life. Often they are based on a false basic assumption: man trusts to have properly informed himself about a particular situation. In truth, it's not like that. During a conversation, the participants take their own background. This becomes problematic when the listener has a different background than the speaker. If important information is omitted, which the speaker ‘clear’ presupposes, this can lead to a transmitter-receiver problem.

Common language patterns that lead to misunderstandings are eradications, generalizations and distortions. The meta-model offers the opportunity to question and respond to uncertainties in the conversation. It consists of a number of questions with which the person experiences more about the underlying depth structure of statements from the surface structure.

Objective: The meta-model allows lost information to be recovered and better understood.


Recovery of lost information

When speaking, we like to leave information, generalize or distort. In this way, a transmitted message often no longer corresponds to the message actually meant. This creates misunderstandings in communication. This makes it necessary to recover this lost information.

If we share our experiences with others, we will meet from the totality of these experiences, a series of partly conscious, but mostly unconscious elections. Within the so-called "depth structure", which would include all the experiences made linguistically to an experience, we select only certain parts which then result in the so-called "surface structure", which we actually communicate verbally to others. So we are able to communicate to others without having to repeat every single perception and experience in detail. In the same way, we also give our experiences a certain value and define their meaning for our lives.

But very often – especially when we have problems – our selection is made in a way that limits our experience and our scope of action. That is, we lose important information about us and our experiences during this filtering process. Because, as already described, we filter information by specific patterns, ‘mount’ here, so to speak, our very private model of the world.

The promised results of this filtering process are often referred to as meta-model violations. In other words, meta-model violations are sentences in which phrases or meanings are missing or experiences are expressed in general or distorted. For the processes with which people filter out the linguistic representation of their world are the same with which they restrict their inner representation of the world.

The Meta model offers ways to question these processes. Winning information here means obtaining a more precise and complete description of the content presented by the speaker through special questions. This process helps to restore the connection between the speaker's language and its full experience.

Presentation of meta-model violations

The three processing processes of eradication, generalization and distortion are presented and explained on the basis of pointed paragraphs.

My favourite style is the telegram style, is also cheaper and enough. I can get out of here. When words like respect, strenge or the essence are swung around his ears and he has to think his part. Then he curses me. But thank God for the meta-model questions!

Meta-model language pattern

The assignment of the language patterns presented below is based on the original: “Metalang und Psychotherapie: The Structure of Magic I” by Richard Bandler and John Grinder. Other publications on the meta-model sometimes find themselves well-greened assignments. In the spirit of NLP there is no ‘real’ or ‘false’.

Repeal

Simple eradications indicate that a spokesman has omitted some elements of his representation of an experience and does not inform the listener of a sentence. Regardless of whether he deliberately or unconsciously omitted it, the result is that the listener lacks this information. If the listener notices the eradication, he can question it and thus gradually reveal further parts of the depth structure.

The main repayments refer to the following elements:

Persons:Who did something?
Object:Who or what was something done?
Room:Where did something happen?
Time:When did something happen?
Method:How was something done?
Options:What were the possibilities?
Number:How many times? How much?

This information is needed at least to reconstruct a context within which the experiences were made and which led to the incomplete model.

  • Repayment of the verb

There is no part of the sentence that belongs to the verb.

A verb can be combined with various details. Some of these details are necessary so that the sentence sounds right for us. Other information is not necessary, but may interest the listener.

Beispiel: <Sie> gibt <mir> <morgen> <in der Stadt> <das Fahrrad>. Bei dem Verb geben müssen nur drei Angaben zum Kontext gemacht werden, nämlich: Wer etwas gibt, was gegeben wird und wem es gegeben wird. Die beiden restlichen Angaben – wann und wo – sind nicht notwendig. Es wäre auch grammatisch korrekt zu sagen: <Sie> gibt <mir> <das Fahrrad>.

Supplied material can be questioned with the following question words: About what? With what? To whom? What exactly? Who? With what? How much?

ExampleQuestion
I am happyWhat are you looking forward to?
Peter is afraid.What is Peter afraid of?
My brother laughed.What did he laugh about?
I bought a bicycle.Who did you buy a bike from?

  • Eradication of the noun

There is a sentence that belongs to a complete sentence with this noun.


ExampleQuestion
Susanne has no idea.What does she have no idea?
He has no time.What's he got no time for?
I have a problem.A problem with who (what)?
The dog is very scared.Who / what is he afraid of?

  • Repayment of adjectives

Adjectives characterize nouns, e.g. large, blue, round, brave etc. In principle, adjectives indicate an eradication. At least that which characterizes the noun in the corresponding manner is always achieved.


ExampleQuestion
He's respected.Who's he looking at?
This man is successful.Where is he successful?
The message is sad.Who makes her sad?
This noise is disturbing.For whom is the noise disturbing?

  • Repayment of Adverb

Adverbs describe how to do something. So they characterize verbs. In this type of eradication, a part of the sentence is missing which belongs to an adverb.


ExampleQuestion
He was reasonable.To whom did he think appropriate?
Obviously, this is a problem.Who is that obvious?
Unfortunately, you can't change it anymore.Who is it regrettable for?
He argued hostile.To whom did he argue hostile?

Adverbs where eruptions can occur are adverbs such as ‘clear’ and ‘open-looking’ or with the ending – wise (e.g.: usual). These eradications can be seen if you have a ‘it is’ in front of the adverb, and the newly shaped sentence gives the same meaning as the old one.

  • Repayment for comparison

The object or the scale to which the comparison refers is missing. Comparative and superlative (enhanced adjectives) refer to a comparison between two experiences or objects. Often, the comparison base is not mentioned by the speaker. The meta-model question to regain the cured material (i.e. the comparison base) is: compared to who? Compared to what?

We distinguish three different levels of comparisons:

  1. Positive comparisons = comparisons in which two properties are compared with one another at the same level, e.g.: large, as large as, small, as small as, as, as old as, etc.
  2. Comparative comparisons = comparisons describing differences, e.g.: larger, larger than, smaller, smaller than, older, older.
  3. Superlative comparisons = comparisons describing maximum levels, e.g.: the largest, the smallest, the smallest, the oldest, the oldest.

ExampleQuestion
The tie was expensive.More expensive compared to what?
Carl Lewis is the fastest.Fastest in terms of what?
The tasks are more difficult.More difficult than what?
I'd rather.Better than what?


  • Repayment by use of modal operators

These include the word groups: may, may, should, it is not possible, able, out of hand. There are modal operators of necessity (must), possibility (can), permission (can) and recommendation (sollen).

Modaloperators are verbs and determine another verb closer. They often point to generalizations that the interlocutor has used in the formation of his world model. That is why they are sometimes assigned to the generalizations.

Our goal is to ask for the consequence or result.

ExampleQuestion
I have to do my homework.What else will happen?
We can't be late.Anything else happens?
You should listen to me now.What happens if I don't listen to you?
We can't do it.What's keeping us from it?
Niemand kann in einem Jahr reich werden.What makes it impossible to become rich in a year?
You can't have more than one woman.What inhibits you to have more than one woman?
I am unable to solve this problem.What prevents you from solving this problem?

Distortion

In the process of distorting, experiences are transformed into different ways. Most of them are twisted in such a way that they strongly limit a person to his possibilities of action.

distortions arise when new events or facts are caused by the glasses of old conclusions or Principles to be perceived. Then the relationships between individual facts or events cannot be perceived unbiased and are apparently logically, but often restrictively related to each other.

  • Nominalizations

In our language it is very common that we make an event from a process. We say for example “I’m glad about my decision.” The word ‘decision’ originally forms the process “decide about something” off. This process has been transformed into a nominalization and now only describes a result. In fact, however, what is described here as a result is a continuous process which can be completely changed or influenced. However, the linguistic formulation with the nominalization does not permit such a view. In other words, nominalizations are an expression of the fact that a process is perceived as something static.

The query can regain updated information and convert nominalizations to process words. This leads to an extension or Completion of Models the world of the speaker and can initiate a transformation process.

ExampleQuestion
My decision not to accept the job is hard in my stomach.What prevents you from deciding differently?
What prevents you from changing your decision?
What would happen if you thought about it again and took the job
I'm full of hope.What are you hoping for?
My conviction has not changed.What are you convinced of?
The frustration is just too big for themWhat is she frustrated about? What exactly does she experience?

If you have difficulty recognizing nominalizations, consider whether you can put the corresponding object in a pusher. A chair can be put in, a decision, conviction, hope, trust, love cannot be put in, because they are nominalizations.

Nominalizations refer to two limitations in the speaker's model:

  • (a) Many elements of the original experience have disappeared from the sentence, they have been blurred;
  • b) Processes have been counteracted, i.e. distorted.

This is why nominalizations in literature are found in the eradications and sometimes in the distortions:

How to denominalize? In order to turn a nominalization back into a process, it is possible to check whether there is a similarly sounding verb, adjective or adverb and to use this in a counter-question: Example: Life is hard. Question: Who lives so that what is hard for?

  • Priorities

Presumptions are sentences that presuppose one or more sentences other than true to make sense. This is about questioning the hidden assumption and thus revealing and relativizing.


ExampleQuestion
If you're so disgusting to me again, I'm not going out with you.What was wrong with you?
Now you've made progress.How do you know I wasn't good before?
If you become wise, you will understand my decision.What makes you think I'm not smart?
What has your attitude changed?What makes her think my attitude has changed?
You're just as selfish as your father.What makes you think my father is selfish?

  • Cause - Effect

The speaking person assumes that an external event or its interlocutor triggers its inner state. You gain the impression that the speaking person has no choice and must experience the feeling.


ExampleQuestion
You're making me angry.How exactly do I make you angry?
The strange woman's eye-catcher distracts me.How exactly does the eye-catcher cause you to be distracted?
You force me to draw consequences.How exactly do I force you
You depress me.How exactly do I depress you?
I'm sad because you forgot me.If I didn't leave you, you wouldn't be sad?
I don't want to be angry, but she doesn't stop criticizing me.Are you always angry when you are criticized?

In fact, however, it is impossible for a person to create feelings in another person. You react with feelings on interactions with others, but you create your feelings yourself. Here the responsibility for feelings is shifted to the outside where they are no longer subject to their own control. Question in such a case the statement so that the responsibility for the reaction is taken over again.

Often it happens that our counterpart believes exactly what we think or feel without having a direct point of reference at all.


ExampleQuestion
I know exactly what you're thinking.How do you know You what I think?
If you loved me, you would do what I expect from you.How do you know I know what you expect from me?
I know what's best for you.How do you know that?
Nie denkst Du an mich.How do you know I never think of you?

Thought reading can consist of interpreting unconscious signals and then pronouncing them. You can often lie right with this, but sometimes also quite beautifully next to it! Why should you make a vague guess if you can ask?

  • Lost Performativ (lost speaker)

A statement about a rule that sounds like it was for the whole world. In this case, the information has been lost to whom or to whom this rule relates or to whom it relates.


ExampleQuestion
It's wrong to kill other people.Who says that?
You don't.Who claims that?
Too much work makes sick.How do you know?

presented by such sentences Your interlocutor's acceptance is valid in his model of the world. Do not automatically take the rules of another person, but check whether it is valid for you.

Generalization

We generalize certain statements because the exceptions are often not important and we do not want to say every time: “It is usually so and so, but exceptions are a) ..., b) ... and c) ....” We often save ourselves and just say: “Always the weather is bad if I want to walk!”, “No one listens to me!”

The problem is, however, that experience is greatly restricted by the generalization. We lose in detail and filling. Sometimes the exceptions are crucial and can help us to gain a new insight.

  • Lack of reference

There is no reference to a specific individual experience so that the statement appears to be true of all experiences. The noun groups are non-specific or nouns are replaced by pronouns. Every noun group is unspecific, from which ‘Wer’ or ‘What’ that you no longer know who or what the spokesperson speaks exactly.

Example: Concrete experience: Sandra has left me.
Generalization: Women don't like me.

ExampleQuestion
Nobody cares what I say.Who exactly does not pay attention to what I say?
What exactly do you say?
We don't want to lose our details.Wer genau soll sich in welchen Details nicht verlieren?
Don't lie.Who shouldn't lie?
Let's stop.Who exactly should stop?

  • Generalized Reference Index

Statements are made about all the elements of a class. The universal quantor is not mentioned, but one can ask the same.


ExampleQuestion
Women are sweet.All women?
Siamcats are very pure.All Siamkatzen?
Men are pigs.All men?

  • Universal quantum

There are maps showing a section from the area but claim to depict the whole area. Statements that are to be valid forever (all time), for everywhere (all places), for each (all people) contain universal quantors. Universal quantors include: always, everyone, all, everywhere, all, never, nothing, no, no.

You can react to universal quantors in two ways:

(a) By using more universal quantors in the speaker's sentence, exaggerating the sentence in such a way that the speaker notices that the generalization is not valid and contradicts the questioner.
Example: I can't trust anyone.
Question: Is there really no one you could ever trust?

(b) You can also ask directly for the original experience (and directly for an example).
Example: I can't trust anyone.
Question: Who was your trust abused by? Who can't trust? Are there some people you can trust?

ExampleQuestion
Nobody cares about me.Really NIEMAND? Nobody at all?
It's impossible to believe anyone.Have you ever had the experience of being able to believe someone?
All people are bad.Really all? Is there no exception?
I never make a mistake.Never? Can you imagine any circumstances where you made a mistake?


  • Symmetrical predicates

They always describe the processes between two people. In this case, the counterpiece also necessarily applies. If Hans fights with me, I always fight with Hans. A dispute always includes two people.


ExampleQuestion
Hans always fights with me.Do I always fight Hans?
My husband doesn't touch me anymore.Are you touching your husband?
She never gives me the hand.Are you handing her?

  • Non-symmetric predicates

This is about activities where only one person is actively involved. In these predicates, the counterpart is not necessarily true, even if it often applies.


ExampleQuestion
The beautiful woman doesn't smile at me.You smile at the beautiful woman?
My father doesn't see me anymore.Do you see your father?

  • If (not) X, then (not) Y

If I don't make X, then Y happens, setting up claims about causal relations that do not necessarily have to apply.


ExampleQuestion
I've got to do good work so the others like me.If others do good things, do you like them?
If I don't love others, no one loves me.If you love other people, do they always love you?

  • Complex equivalence

a verifiable experience is linked to an interpretation as if one would automatically mean the other. Here, two syntactically similar sentences are expressed shortly in succession, either first the experience and then the interpretation or vice versa.

Reaction possibilities on complex equivalences:
Is that always?
Does X always mean...Y?
Do you know a case by not meaning Y?
What does it mean for you to have X?
If not X, then not Y?
If X, then not Y?

ExampleQuestion
You are interested Not for me because you don't look at me when I talk to you.Does "I don't look at you" always mean "I don't care for you"?
My boss doesn't take me seriously. He always smiles like I feel laughed. Does your boss never take you seriously when he smiles? If you smile at your boss, does that mean you don't take him seriously?
You don't bring me flowers anymore. You don't love me anymore.So flowers love and no flowers don't mean love?

  • Completely specified verbs

Verbs are used which describe the events very generally. Basically, almost all the verbs used are incomplete. Even if I say: “Ina kissed Mario on his mouth” there would be much more to say, for example how long, how intense. Therefore, the incompletely specified verbs are sometimes assigned to the eradications.


ExampleQuestion
We came together.How exactly did you come together?
My sister always overlooks me.How exactly does your sister overlook you?
Klaus went to Bremen.How exactly did Klaus go to Bremen?
My cat calls for attention.How exactly did her cat demand attention?

Attention meta-model monsters!

The meta model is super helpful to get into the depth structure of a behavior or a problem. Various questioning techniques are available for this purpose - but not every question is targeted. In the NLP trainings 22 question techniques are taught and sometimes all questions appear super. It is therefore necessary to choose the questions carefully and not to become the meta-monster. Anyone who masters the meta-model knows when which question fits and which questions are superfluous.

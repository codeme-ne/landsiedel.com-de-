Introduction and Function

Introduction

Metaphors are incredibly important for communication and their own understanding. Since ancient times, metaphors have been used as means of teaching and changing ideas, ideas and attitudes to life. Shamans, philosophers and prophets have intuitively recognized the force inherent in metaphors and served themselves. From Platon to Jesus, from Buddha to Don Juan to Richard Bandler, metaphors were recognized and used as powerful influencing tools.

In metaphors, people, animals or plants face certain difficulties or are in special situations. The metaphor tells how to solve the problem, how to deal with the situation. So we learn something and maybe the metaphor meets us and gives us an idea how to solve our problems.

Function and benefits

  • The metaphor delivers game material for the unconscious. The red thread of the metaphor gives the left brain time, and the message goes directly into the unconscious; the left brain has no access to it.
  • The metaphor can also be used well in coaching, because it offers a charm to think about something in a new way. The current problem is translated into a different context and can then be viewed with more distance.
  • The metaphor can often be incredibly helpful in integration. A dead end is triggered by a sudden insight. Even if the metaphor does nothing but to translate information for the other half of the brain.
  • The participants of seminars are much more familiar and more familiar after a metaphor has been told.
  • Metaphors increase motivation and mood. You can be well used to put someone else in a certain emotional state.

Important principles of metaphors

A) Pacing and Leading

Cameron Bandler tells the following example, in which the current experience and the desired experience will soon be blurred and thus the Pacen and Leads fluently merges:

"An attractive woman named Dot came to counsel. She wanted to learn to control her celebrity, so she was looking for help. She was married to a good man (so her description) and had two dear children, but, whenever and with whom it was possible to engage in extramarital relationships. She wanted to dismiss this behavior.

I used the following elements in their shielding to build a therapeutic metaphor. Like so many attractive women, Dot was concerned about their slim figure (although it was not too thick at all), and therefore I used this content to make the metaphor appear like a natural expansion of our therapeutic interaction."

Therapeutic Metapher

This celebrity leads her to the loss of her husband and her self-respect. A woman on the way to obesity. Dot cannot resist the temptation that other men represent for them.
A woman who can't resist nutritious desserts and good food if she eats out. Dot finds extramarital sex more exciting. This woman loves eating out of the house. Dot is dissatisfied with sexual relations in her marriage. This woman just stumbles around in her own homemade food.

Every extramarital experience produces more guilt and brings it closer to the loss of her husband. Every externally fed meal produces more fat. Dot's guilt gets so painful that she has to do something about it. She can't sleep at night, etc. The thick lady has to do something against her habits. She doesn't fit in her clothes anymore. Dot had never developed satisfying sexual behavior together with her husband. The thick lady had never learned to cook something beautiful for herself.

So far, each element in the constructed metaphor is isomorphic (i.e. there is a one-to-one relationship in the structure) to the present problem. The elements reflect the present problem by their form analogous to the problem. The next step is to move from the reflection of the problem to a solution at the level of behavior.

The desired reaction to which the metaphor is based is that Dot changes its behavior, so that there is a problem solution. The story must therefore make a change of behaviour in the fat-sufficient woman, which is metaphorically represented by Dot.

Dot should use energy to create stimulating and satisfying sexual experiences with her husband. The woman was trying to change the kitchen. She began reading cookbooks to find suitable dishes, and began experimenting with preparing healthy and cosy meals.

Dot should find the necessary satisfaction at home. With the time, faster than one would suspect, she found that there was nothing in the restaurants that was sufficient for her own domestic creations, and she no longer had the need to stuff herself elsewhere, as she now found her satisfaction at home. Dot is proud of her marriage and her sexual relationship with her husband. Slender and rank as this woman is now, the once-strong woman is as proud of her own culinary skills as well as her taut figure.

People react to such metaphors without trying. Something happens, but they often don't know exactly what.

Metaphoric solution

B) Double induction

For double induction, it is characteristic that one person of two speakers simultaneously tells one or more metaphors or Suggestions or instructions are given simultaneously (also combined). A speaker for his auditive input uses the right ear of this person and the other for his auditive input uses the left ear. The aim is to overload the conscious perception to transport information directly into the unconscious. Each message is processed by the opposite brain half, which leads to different experiences in the two body halves.

C) Hidden Stories

The metaphorical image of complex relationships or mediation steps is the work with the so-called "patched stories", which are also called "stacked realities" or "Nested Loops". From the construction, you can use a story in which a story is embedded... etc. Switching or changing from one to another takes place at a time of relative tension in the current history.

Start

1. Beginne Geschichte A

Two. Beginning History B

3. Beginning History C

4. Ending History C

Five. Beende Geschichte B

6. Beende Geschichte A

7. End

The closing of the stories thus takes place like the drying of washed plates. Just as one finishes the last started story first, the last cleaned plate is taken from the stack to dry it.

Constructions of metaphors


  1. Determine the problem:
    What is it about? Who are the relevant persons? What role do they play?
    How is the main person? How do others react?
    Prepare a problem description.
  2. Target:
    What does the person who has the problem want to achieve? How do the relevant other persons react to the behaviour?
    Keep this solution in writing.
  3. Select the appropriate content level:
    Search a level of content that could reflect the problem structure, e.g. sky of the gods, demons' kingdom, fairy tales with princes, princesses, magicians, witches and fairies, talking animals, plants and stones, myths and figures from the realm of science fiction, great personalities of history.
    Important: The content level must be interesting for the addressee of the metaphor.
  4. Problem and target in the content levels reflect:
    History must be structurally similar to the problem. The therapeutic metaphor basically contains the structure of the problematic situation of the client, its relationships and the problem context. Then the structures and processes of achievement of goals in the medium of history are to be mirrored.
  5. Design the way to the destination:
    Bringing resources and building the feeling that the client is able to cope with the problem. Keep the way to the goal in writing.
  6. Write the metaphor.
    Please leave one line free.
  7. The metaphor ecologically examine.
    Check the ecology of the target. The aim fits into a human life context without having to draw negative consequences for the personality structure of the person concerned and its social context. Unusual and dangerous insights should be excluded. Check the metaphors for inconveniences that can be cured and possible interpretations and conclusions that should not be considered.
  8. Install feedback instructions:
    Integrate possibilities to provoke physiological signals, so that it can be recognized whether the listener "is going along".
  9. Refine the metaphor

    • Insert improvements
    • Use NLP patterns for the way to the destination
    • Mirrors and guiding with Representation systems:
      Pacing the main representation system,
      Lead into new representation systems to expand perception.
    • Mirrors and guiding of satir categories:
      More important, accuser, rationalizer, deflector.

Beware of misunderstandings in metaphors

"A very competent woman who worked in a social-therapeutic community wanted a schizophrenic woman to spend more time in the dayroom so that she would be more in contact with others and spend less time in isolation. So she told her a story about a beautiful rose that bloomed at the back in a shady, damp corner of a backyard. One day, the gardener noticed this rose, cut it off and put it in a vase in the entrance hall where everyone who went past could see and admire her ...
The next day the young woman cut the pulsed veins to get attention (just like the gardener had cut the rose)!"

One cannot stop a hundred percent from finding a kind of interpretation from which one does not want him to do it; but you can at least be so careful that you make it hard to hit the wrong way. Therefore, check your metaphors with unintentional meanings, ambiguities, assumptions and possible interpretations. Of course, a good metaphor also lives from these processes.

Phrases like "dispense the spoon" have two meanings: a literal and that someone dies. Every time you need such a phrase, both meanings are registered.

Exercise for metaphors


  1. A finds a state that he wants to change and finds a corresponding target state that he wants to achieve instead.
  2. B now asks A on the basis of the list to the initial state: "If this state, this problem, this feeling... (here use the corresponding terms of the list) ..., what kind of .... (e.g. landscape) ... would it then?"
    Find five to ten or more metaphors for this state and then proceed with the target state.
  3. B now invents a story with the found terms. It begins with the initial situation and then passes the corresponding metaphors elegantly into the target state within history.

The transfer from one to the other can take place in a variety of ways:

  • a flying carpet
  • die Hauptperson steigt in ein Flugzeug
  • she dreams of a dream
  • another person appears and tells a story etc.

Metaphers can be used in almost any context, here 5 applications:

Metaphern konstruieren in 7 Schritten

  1. Setting a problem
    What is this problem about? Who are the relevant persons involved in this problem? Who plays what role? How is the person who has the problem?
  2. Define target
    What would the problem-affected person want to achieve? How can she behave? How does she feel when she reaches her goal?
  3. Choose suitable content level
    Look for a content level where you can mirror the problem structure. Everything is conceivable, depending on the taste. Fairy tales, fables, science fiction, ...
  4. Problem und Ziel in der Inhaltsebene spiegeln
    Should a story solve problems, it must be structurally similar to the problem. The therapeutic metaphor basically contains the structure of the problematic situation of the client, its relationship and the problem context.
  5. Designing the way to the destination
    How does the hero or the hero of history come to the finish? Consider resources that could also be used by the addressee of your metaphor for target achievement.
  6. Write the metaphor
    Let your imagination run free and observe the structure.
  7. Check the ecology of the metaphor
    Ecologically, we call a goal if it fits into a human being's life context, without having negative consequences for the personality structure of the person concerned and its social context.

Tips for the presentation of metaphors in the training context

Use body language to see the listeners what you say. The difference between INNEN and AUSSEN moments. In an important action or reaction, always enter the story and dig this moment instead of describing it. Metapher life.

EXTERNAL: Talk to the audience directly. Make eye contact with the audience. Talk with a telling voice in the past form.

INNs: Don't talk to the audience directly. Don't make eye contact with the audience. They are here and now in an imagined, imagined reality. They speak in the present form. Show personal, private behavior.

The distribution of INNEN and AUSSEN moments should be 20 to 80. The INNEN-moments are like spice. You can't take too much of it. Imagine the INNEN-moments as a cayenne pepper. Too much is too much.

Body language, movement and gesture

If you say:
“I bend to take my bag” – bend and take the imaginary bag.
“I pushed my toe at the desk edge” – push your toe and feel the pain.
“I went and opened the door” – go a few steps and open the door pantomily.

In every context: Set your Voice so that your listeners hear and feelwhat you say.
Use fast and slow response phases.

  • Proceedings shows: stress or fear that you are at the end of your power, disappointment or despair, Impatience or haste, anger or anger.
  • Slowly speaking shows: shock or unbelief, boredom or lethargy, fear or concern, controlled anger, exhaustion or fatigue, Task or defeat.

The four satir types (survivals)

Virginia Satir has discovered four categories of communication or attitudes that people accept when they are under tension. Each of these categories of satir is characterized by a special posture, a special gesture, accompanying body feelings and a specific syntax.

1. Applicants

It is one of the four most important ways we react when we feel that our survival is threatened.
When we appease, we disregard our own feelings about our value, override our power someone else and say to everything. A person who tends to appease others and the context, but his own true feelings are disregarded.

It is important to be pleasing, which is very appreciated in most cultures and families.
But the appalling differs from the congruent effort to make someone happy. We appease at the expense of our own value. When we complain, we disregard our self-esteem and send the message to the others that we are not important.

Substantiating reaction

  • Words: agreeing ("Whatever you want is fine. I exist only to make you happy."
  • Body: Reconcilable ("I am helpless."
  • Thoughts and feelings: "I feel like nothing; without him I am dead. I'm not worth anything."

The reconciliation always speaks in a smiling way; he tries to please; he apologises and never opposes anything, no matter what. He's a Ja-Sager.

He says he can't do anything for himself. He always has to find someone who recognizes him. You will later notice that if you play this role only five minutes, you will be bad and you want to break. In order to be really appalling, it helps to give yourself the idea that you are not worth anything.

You can feel happy that you are allowed to eat at all. You owe everyone thanks, and you're really responsible for everything that goes wrong. You know you could've prevented the rain if you needed your head, but you don't have one. Of course you will agree to any criticism of you. You are, of course, grateful for the fact that someone speaks to you at all, no matter what or how he says. You wouldn't come to the idea of asking for something for yourself. After all, who are you that you could make demands?

Besides, if you were just good enough, it would come from yourself. Be the most tacky, most suffering, feet kissing person you can only be. Imagine kneeling your body with a leg, a bit shaking, a hand-getting stretched out, and make sure your head is strongly upwards, and you get headaches in no time. When you speak in this position, your voice will be waving and pussy, because you keep your body so licked that you don't have enough air for a rich, full voice. You will say "yes" to everything, no matter what you feel or think.This appalling figure is the posture that corresponds to the reconciling reaction form.

Two. Indictments

Indictments are the exact opposite of the appalling.
The accusing attitude is an incongruent reflection of the societal rule that we should enter for ourselves and not accept any excuses, inconveniences or deceptions from whom - in short, that we must not be "weak".

To protect ourselves, we attack other people or the circumstances and sue them.
When we complain, the others do not count for us, but only ourselves and the context. If we have made ourselves the attitude of the indictor, we are often referred to as hostile, tyrannical, nörglerisch or violent.

Indictment reaction

  • Words: not agreeing ("you never do something right. What's wrong with you?")
  • Body: accusing, demanding ("I am the boss here.")
  • Thoughts and feelings: "I am lonely and unsuccessful."

The indictor is a "error searcher", a dictator, a boss. He acts superbly, and he seems to say, "If you were not there, everything would be fine." Inside, the muscles and organs feel tense. Blood pressure is increasing. The voice is hard, firm, often shrill and loud. Convincing "injuries" requires you to be as loud and tyrannical as you can. Finish everything and everyone.

To be accustomed, it is helpful to imagine that you have an incriminatingly stretched finger, and to begin with the sentences: "You never do it," or "You always do it," or "why do you always," "why do you never do" and so on. Don't worry about the answers. It's not important.

Rather, the plaintiff is trying to figure out his weight than really find out. Whether you know about it or not, if you're accusing, you breathe in small, narrow trains or keep your breath completely on because your neck muscles are so tight. Have you ever seen a really first-class accuser whose eyes were swollen, neck muscles and nasal wings that were red and whose voice sounded like someone who slapped coal?

Imagine yourself with a hand on your hip, the other arm with a straight index finger. Your face is distorted, your lips crippled, your nose wings vibrate when you call out snippets and criticize everything under the sun. You don't really think you're worth something. If you find someone who obeys you, then you feel at least something.

3. Deviating (Irrelevant Reacting)

The fourth survival is the irrelevant reaction that is often confused with amusement or clown games. The irrelevant pattern is the counterpart to the excessive-rational. When people act irrelevant, they are constantly moving.

It is an attempt to distract the attention of others from the subjects under discussion.

The irrelevant reactant constantly produces new ideas and wants to do myriads of things at the same time.

Even, others and context of interaction play no role for such people when they are in the attitude of irrelevant.

The public often calls people who act irrelevantly as spontaneous and cheerful. Often, in life from irrelevant behavior, a general bodilyness develops, and they are aimless. As long as they manage to distract the attention of topics that are only at least associated with stress for them, they believe that they will succeed in survival. They are not able to focus on a particular topic.

Deviating reaction

  • Words: without relationship, insane, the words make no sense
  • Body: angular and pointing in different directions.
  • Thoughts and feelings: "No one makes me feel something. I don't belong anywhere."

Whatever the distractor says or does, it has no relation to what anyone else says or does. He never answers a question directly. Inside he feels dizzy or blurred. The voice can be a singing song and often does not match the words; it can move up and down without cause because it is directed at nothing.

If you play this distracting role, it will help you imagine that you have a sloping head that always turns, as you do not know where you are going, and do not notice when you arrive. You're too busy moving your mouth, eyes, arms and legs. Make sure you never use specific words. Ignore everyone's questions, possibly respond with a separate question on a completely different subject. Pull an imaginary fiber out of someone's clothing, tie laces on us so much.

Imagine your body as if it proves in different directions. Make overtrial X legs by putting the knees together. This will push your buttocks back and help to bend your back and bring your arms and legs in different directions. At first, this role seems to be facilitated, but after a few minutes of play, the horrible loneliness and purposeless feelings become clear. If you move fast enough, you won't notice it so much.

Take the four physical attitudes to your own exercise. Keep 60 seconds and note what happens to you.

4. Rationalization

The communication pattern of excessive rationalization makes both the self and the other person unconsidered.
To be excessively rational means that one is only directed at the context, usually at the level of information and logic. In order to make us aware of the excessively rational attitude, we are stiffly erected and immovable, both arms being on the body sides or being symmetrically bound in front of the body. We will soon get severe back pain because we are too rigid.

Our feet are close to each other. Since we don't move a miene, our face is completely unpressurized. When someone talks to us, we docile long and wide, seemingly eise and dignified.
The obvious feature of this attitude is an almost inhuman objectivity. We neither admit ourselves nor others to focus on feelings. This reflects the social convention, maturity means not moving, not looking, touching nothing and feeling no feelings.

Rational reaction

  • Words: extremely reasonable ("When carefully observed, one might notice the processed hands of one present here."
  • Body: unmoved, stretched ("I am calm, cool and collected").
  • Thoughts and feelings: ("I feel lightly delivered."

The rationalizer is very correct and very reasonable without showing the appearance of a feeling. It is quiet, cool and collected. He could be compared to a computer or a reference book. The body feels dry, often cool and unreliable. The voice is dry and monotonous; the words sound slightly abstract.

If you want to be rational, use the longest words that are possible even if you are not sure about their meaning. At least that's how you'll listen intelligently. After a paragraph no one will listen.

To put yourself in the mood of this role, imagine that your spine would be a long, heavy rod extending from your back to the neck, and you would have a 30 cm wide iron collar around your neck. Keep everything on you as motionless as possible, even your mouth. It'll be hard for you to hold your hands still, but try. If you rationalize, your voice will gradually die, because you have no feeling from the skull down. Your brain is constantly struggling that nothing moves on you, and you are busy choosing the right words. After all, you must never make a mistake.

The sadness of this role is that it seems to be an ideal for many people. Say the right words: don't show a feeling, don't react."

Exercise to Satir - Categories

  1. Exercise in groups of four: Simulated should be a controversial situation: e.g. for and against the mastery of nuclear power, the "Sulen-Ehe", Catholic priests, opportunities to cope with unemployment etc. The four satir types are to be occupied.
  2. Each participant slips into a role and discusses as a Placater, Blamer, Computer, Distractor about five minutes; then all change the role until each position has taken once.
  3. After a complete passage, the participants exchanged: What role was the most unpleasant? What role was (relative) easy to take? How was the effect on the others? How did the others work with their roles?
  4. In the next phase, counter strategies are to be tested. One in the group slips into the role he wants to explore even more for five minutes. Its task is to give the group a daily problem in the self-selected role. The other participants are trying to carefully question this role allocation and to extend deflated, distorted or generalized positions of perception. Every role should be taken once.
  5. Final feedback and group exchange.

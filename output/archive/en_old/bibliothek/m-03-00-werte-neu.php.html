Values and rules of life

Values find your expression in terms such as security, love, honesty, honor, loyalty, reliability, responsibility, etc. They are behind our behaviors and are internal motives. If you want to change your behavior, but have values that are contrary to the desired behavior, you will not succeed. You won't be able to keep your new behavior long.

Only if your values support your behavior, you will be able to permanently change your life. Because from our values we also form our Living rules.

What are values?

Definition: As a rule formulated as abstract nominalizations, values are of utmost importance both for individuals and companies and for society. Values are the driving force and orientation for our behavior, and serve as criteria for the subsequent evaluation or assessment of our actions. Values constitute an ideal reference framework that guides our activities and assessments and focuses on achieving the desired results. They must be in a concrete framework of interaction and be "treated" there, because they form the basis for our judgments on what makes life worth living.

Every person uses his personal values to move towards his goals and results. The feeling of personal satisfaction and completeness results in a match between current behavior and personal values. The people you love or are friends with, the way you educate your children (wore), the political direction you support, how you perform your work, the clothes you wear, the food you eat - all of this is determined by the individual values you use for you.

Species

Social values: Basic values on which a society is based (standards).
Examples: Security, Freedom, Justice, Equality and Progress

Ethical values: Everyone has his own ethical values, according to which he acts and evaluates his surroundings.
Examples: Success, Respect, Honesty, Health

With a view to Motivational factorscan be divided as follows:

  • Change: freedom (thanks, action), stimulation (emotional, intellectual) etc.
  • Reinforcement personally: success, power, matter etc.
  • Strengthening social: care, justice, tolerance, love etc.
  • Preservation: regularity, tradition, nature protection, health etc.

Origin

In addition to ethics, values are also an important aspect in sociology, pedagogy, psychology and theology. Values are often not rational, but rather emotional, impulsive, aesthetic, moral or religious. With the help of values, we judge what is good/bad, pleasant/nearly, true/false, beautiful/ugly or appropriate/unreasonable.

Since values are very personal, strongly emotional orientation principles and motivators and include our beliefs, the same development periods apply to their development as for the appropriation of belief systems:
The time of "unconscious modeling" by introjection and identification with the immediate educators follows a time of out-of-familiary socialization in school, the group of the same-aged and the workstation; Finally, values can also be integrated by conscious modeling.

Sources of the development of our values are: our family of origin, our friends, the community in which we grow up, social institutions (school, church, associations etc.), the working environment and the economic, social and political conditions. Values are thus strongly connected to our identity and beliefs and attitudes.

Values are the most unconscious parts of our personality. Values are principles, standards or qualities that are accepted as valuable or desired by individuals or groups.

Values occur in close connection with the beliefs, attitudes and meta programs of the individual person. The terms: honor, responsibility, friendship, love, creativity etc. may be values and criteria that serve a person as orientation patterns and sorting principles of circumstances and activities in every context. True facts and actions are sorted out by our five senses and personal activities are aimed at achieving the desired values, with different persons of a community, an institution, or an enterprise, having different values and criteria. Faith in certain things can also express itself in the form of values. Thus faith in religion or a certain God can also be a value.

What are my values?

Values and criteria for the individual can be found by observing the nominalizations used or by simple questions such as:

  • "What has prompted you to accept your current job?"
  • "What is the most important thing in your social relations?"
  • "Was erachtest Du als besonders wertvoll in Deinem Leben?"
  • "Why are you doing this?"

Individual work


  1. What projects, plans, projects, leisure activities have You've been done during the last 2-5 years? Create a list, find out the corresponding values and enter them into a percentage division.
  2. What projects, plans, projects, leisure activities want You'll be performing during the next 2-5 years? Create a list and find out the corresponding values. Enter these into a percentage division.

(Alternatively or in addition to 1 and 2, you could also ask: "How do you spend your time?" "What are you spending your money for?" "What are you investing energy for?" The more precisely these questions are answered, the more we know about the real preferences and thus indirectly about the act-leading values. What a person knows about his values will always be a hopeless mixture of rationalization, idealized self-image and some true self-observations).

Example

The values could be, for example, passion, imagination, freedom, development, adventure, success and health.

Now I wonder what value is more important to me.

Is passion or imagination more important to me? Passion.

Is passion more important than freedom? Yeah.

What is more important: passion or development? ...

This is how I compare all values in turn with passion. If there is no value that is more important than passion, passion comes first. If there is a value more important, it is preferred and compared with all others. If, for example, success is more important than passion, then success number 1. If you go through this consistently, you have quickly brought your highest values into a range.

Difficulties in classification

At some points it can be that the classification is difficult for you.

Example: Is freedom more important or adventure? When I experience adventure, I feel free; without freedom, I cannot experience adventures.

Proposal: If you don't get any further, ask yourself, "What does freedom and adventure mean for me?"

Freedom could mean doing and letting what you want. Adventure could mean addressing exciting challenges. Now you can better compare: What is more important for me: doing and letting what I want, or mastering exciting challenges? To master exciting challenges - adventure is therefore a higher value. If you still can't make a clear distinction despite demand, ask yourself, "What would happen if one of the two values fell away." Could I imagine a life without adventure? It would be boring, I'd be energyless and have little incentive. How about I have no freedom? I'd be dependent. Is it worse to be dependent or energyless? Energyless. This makes adventure more important than freedom. Wouldn't it be better for me to have freedom, but adventures? Or is it more pleasant to miss out on the adventures and to have freedom?

Change of value hierarchy


  1. Identify a hierarchy of personal values
  2. Decision of the change in value hierarchy: Find out with the interlocutor what of his personal values he wants to change in the position of the elaborate hierarchy.
  3. Change of values hierarchy: Find the Sub-modes, Meta programs, Strategies, Representation systems the most important value and the value to be changed.
    Use the submodalities of the most important value and transfer them to the value to be changed. Check whether meta programs, strategies etc. have already changed. If not, use the change of meta programs and strategies to support this process.
  4. Creation of a new hierarchy of personal values: Let the communication partner reorder its hierarchy of personal values. How would you now arrange your personal values? What is the changed value now?

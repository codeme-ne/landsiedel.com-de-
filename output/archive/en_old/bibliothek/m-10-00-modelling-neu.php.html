NLP modelling

Introduction to Modelling

Modelling is the NLP discipline. If we think back to the history of the NLP, then all these strategies and techniques have emerged when one observed excellence of excellent grains, in small Chunks to describe it in a way that allows others to follow it.


On this topic, our online academy has a 75-minute ride from an online seminar that you can watch in full length right away. Just click on the "to the video" button to find out more.


Online seminar - Modelling weekend

The weekend is for people who want to understand themselves: only if you know who you are, you can also know what you want!

Modelling is to understand and understand others. Modelling is to learn from each other. And modeling is to recognize your own strengths. We will therefore take a model from the NLP on the first day, adapt it to you, develop a tailor-made image of your qualities and then work with this model. On the second day we do the same with other people – both with other participants in small groups and with external models.



You can say with Dilts:

"NLP is the process by which the relevant behavioural components of those communicatively particularly exceptionally gifted people were discovered and organized into a working model.

.... NLP has developed techniques and distinguishing criteria that identify and describe patterns of verbal and nonverbal behavior.

.... The aim of the NLP modeling process is not that a correct or true description of the thinking process of a specific person emerges, but rather to create an instrumental map that allows us to apply the modeled strategies in any useful way."



After Walker:

"When modeling, an attempt is made to visualize a process in such a way that it is possible for third parties to practice this process."

Modeling can be carried out in many ways. The spectrum ranges from unconscious and informal modeling (which we constantly do with others) to challenging, complex modeling strategies of excellence in various areas. Modelling is a natural process. Children intuitively model their parents and other people, and thus learn complex behaviors, attitudes and perspectives in an unconscious way.

When modeling, it is crucial to what level or Chunk size of skills you want to focus on. Sometimes simple imitation or mirroring is enough when it comes to simple behavioral skills. However, this is ineffective when it comes to complex cognitive and linguistic skills.

The most commonly used methods for eliciting in the NLP are:


  1. remembering and rediscovering a specific experience or
  2. performing a task that requires or triggers a specific ability, strategy, resource or problem condition.

For example, if a person is an excellent speaker, they could be:

  • (a) bitten, an eine Gelegenheit zu denken, als er oder sie eine Rede hielt, oder
  • (b) ask to sit and talk before a group.

Often different terms are used: English modeling, modeling process, modeling or German modeling.

Why can you use modeling?


  1. Improvement of meta-cognition:
    To understand something better, for example, to teach it.
  2. Improving performance:
    so that you can repeat something and refine it until it is really good, for example: athlete. The top experts will be taken into account.
  3. Achieving a specific outcome:
    e.g. spelling, control of phobias or diseases. The successful cases or examples are examined.
  4. Transfer to other content:
    Processes useful in a living area or context can be transferred to another area.

Three basic perspectives for modeling

In a successful modeling, a certain phenomenon or appearance from several perspectives is looked at. Here the three positions of perception from the NLP-Practitioner come to us very much help.

The first position represents its own perspective - the situation is experienced fully associated with itself.

The second position is the perspective of the other - you look at yourself and the situation dissociated by yourself.

In der Position is an unparticipated observer and thus adopts an external perspective.

  • Modeling from the first position means trying something yourself and going after it, in what way we do something. We see, hear and feel from our own perspective.
  • Modeling from the second position means "to stand in the shoes" of the person who is modeled, and to try to think and act as far as possible as this other person. This can convey important intuitive insights about significant but unconscious aspects of the thinking and action of the modeled person.
  • Modeling from the third position would mean that you are resigned and watched as unbiased witness how the person to be modeled interacts with others (including ourselves). In the third position we disregard our personal judgment and pay attention only to what our senses perceive, how a natural scientist would investigate a certain phenomenon through a telescope or through a microscope.
  • The Position would mean a kind of intuitive synthesis of all these perspectives to get a feeling for the whole "form".

Implicites and explicit modeling

It can be described as a function of two basic dimensions:

Consciousness (knowledge) and competence (trade). It is possible to know or understand an activity, but incapable of performing it (conscious incompetence). It is also possible to be able to perform an activity well, but not know how to do it (unconscious competence). Mastery of skill means both the ability to "do what you know" and to "know what you do".

Cognitive and behavioral skills can be either implicit or explicitly modeled. Implizites Modelling means going primary to the second position with the person who is to be modeled to personally get an intuitive feeling for what a subjective experience makes them. Explicit modeling means going to a third person to describe the explicit structure of the experience of the role model, so that it is transferable to others.

Basic phases of modeling (from Dilts: Modelling with NLP)

The following basic phases of the typical NLP modeling process reflect the path from implicit to explicit modeling, which was applied to Bandler in Grinder's original proposal:

Preparation

To prepare modeling, (1) the selection of a person who has the ability to model and (2) to define the following:

  • den Kontext, in dem das Modelling stattfinden soll
  • where and when you want access to the person to be modeled
  • which relationship you want to have with the person to be modeled
  • in welchem Zustand man beim Modelling sein wird

It also includes the corresponding conditions, Anchor and to set up "rescue lines" which will allow you to fully devote yourself to the project.

Phase 1: Unconscious entry

In the first phase of the modeling process, the person who is to be modeled involves an example of the desired power or capability within a corresponding context. Start modeling by going to the second position to intuitively get a feeling for the skills that the person shows. This is done without looking for any special patterns. Rather, simply take in the attitude and physiology of the model and try to identify you internally with him or her.

Sometimes it is better to take over the micro-muscle movements of the person concerned instead of reflecting the obvious actions of the person. The obvious behavior of the model is the surface structure. Micro-muscle movements and changes in the second position will allow you to advance to the underlying depth structure. (In addition, obvious mirroring can sometimes distract the people you want to model.)

This is the phase of "unconscious entry". Do not try to understand what the model does. Building filters can cause you to lose important information. You don't know what's important and what's not. In this phase it is often useful to assume a state of "noth-not-knowing". This is a state in which all previous mental maps and assumptions are put aside in relation to their own current experience. When someone puts himself in a state of "non-knowledge", he or she tries to drop all previous assumptions and to form a fresh and unbiased opinion of a particular situation or experience.

If you notice that in the second position you have developed a good intuitive feel for the person you model, bring you into a context where you can apply the examined skill. Start trying the skills within this context as if you were the person you modeled. Then try to achieve the same results by being yourself.

Dies wird Dir eine sogenannte doppelte Beschreibung der betreffenden Fertigkeit vermitteln, die Du modellierst. Wenn Du in etwa dieselben Reaktionen hervorrufst wie das Modell, ist die erste Modelling-Phase abgeschlossen.

Phase 2 - The Subtraction Process

The next step in the modeling process is to sort out what is essential for the behavior of the model, what is irrelevant. (For example, you don't have to sit in a wheelchair and wear a purple pyjama like Milton Erickson to achieve similar therapeutic results with hypnosis as he does.) In this phase, you begin to explicitly formulate the strategies and behaviors you have modeled. Since you are able to cause similar reactions as the person you modeled, you will also want to use your own behavior in the first position as a reference point. (That means playing the skill as "manself" instead of "as if" one would be the one you modeled.)

Your goal is to clarify and define the specific cognitive and behavioral steps required for the desired results in the selected context/s. At this stage, you will gradually systematically leave parts of each of the behaviors or strategies you have identified to see what really makes a difference.

Everything you leave and what you get for the reactions you do not make any difference is not essential for the model. If you leave something that makes a difference for the results you have achieved, you have found something essential for the model. This procedure is called "subtraction process". Its purpose is to reduce the modeled steps to its simplest and most elegant form and to distinguish the essence of superstition.

When you complete this phase, you will have your current "minimum model" of how you can reproduce the skills of the model yourself (i.e. in your first position). You will also have your intuitive insights from your second position about the skills of the model you have developed by putting yourself in its or its position. In addition, you will have a view from the third position that allows you to recognize the difference between the way you reproduce the model's skills, and the very own way this person shows the ability. This is called a triple description in the NLP.

Phase 3 - design

In the final phase of modeling, a context and a procedure are designed to enable other people to learn the modeled skills and thus enable them to achieve the results that were available to the person acting as a model. For this design, you should synthesize the information you received from all three positions of perception.

Instead of simply imitating the individual steps that the modeled person went through, it is generally most effective to provide the learners with corresponding reference experiences, with the help of which they can discover and develop the special "circuit" they need in order to achieve the right skills. In order to acquire the skill, it is not necessary to run through the same modeling process you have run through.

Different learners will have different conscious and unconscious competences as "output states". You should include this in your draft. For example, if a certain method that you have modeled requires visualization, some learners may already be able to do this quite well while others may be completely new. Some learners will be able to combine several steps in the process to form a single one, while others have to divide a certain step into smaller subtasks.

Again: The guiding principle is the "usefulness" of your design for the learners for whom the model is intended.

Determining features in modeling - process

The most important features or distinctions that are respected in the NLP modeling process are:


  1. Physiology
    Observing body construction, posture patterns, gestures, symmetry and type of movement, eye movements and other access instructions, such as verbal patterns such as voice position and speech speed (the B.A.G.E.L. model).
  2. Cognitive strategies
    Observe the preference of certain sensor-specific representation systems, submodality patterns and habitual cognitive sequences (the R.O.L.E. model).
  3. Meta Program Pattern
    Observe general organisational patterns such as time perception and management, relationships with close, target orientation etc.
  4. Faith and Value Systems
    Observe all expressed values, rules, attitudes and assumptions about the behaviour to be modeled or the skill in question.
  5. Meta pattern
    Beobachten der Interaktion zwischen der Person, die modelliert wird, und den anderen Personen, mit denen sie in der Situation, die Sie modellieren, zu tun hat. Achte auf alle Muster in der Kommunikation und in den Beziehungen zwischen der modellierten Person und den anderen, die mit der Situation zu tun haben.

Of course, some of these criteria will be more relevant to modeling some skills than others.

Simple behavioral modeling, for example, will most likely focus on certain patterns in physiology. On the other hand, modeling simple cognitive skills will generally focus on representation systems and submodalities. Modeling a complex behavioral capability would require more weight on interactive meta patterns and so on.

The following list shows which NLP distinguishing categories generally refer to the skill level that is at the heart of a specific modeling project:

  • simple behavioral - special physical hints and actions
  • simply cognitive - representation systems and submodalities
  • simply linguistic - meta-model patterns and predicates
  • complex behavior-specific - S.C.O.R.E. model differences and perception positions
  • complex cognitive - S.O.A.R. model differences, meta program patterns and logical levels
  • komplex linguistisch - Kommunikations-Matrix-Unterscheidungen und Sleight of Mouth-Muster.

Step sequence of a modeling process


  1. Conducting a need analysis to determine the specific topics, contexts and skills to be addressed.
  2. Select the persons to be modelled.
  3. Create modeling scenarios and perform modeling procedures to update the skills or services to be examined and to collect the necessary information.
  4. Erkennen relevanter Muster im Verhalten, in den Strategien und Principles etc. of individuals who were modeled.
  5. Organize the patterns that were discovered to a descriptive and teaching structure, i.e. to a model.
  6. Experimental testing and refining the model by testing it in the relevant contexts to see whether it leads to the desired results.
  7. Develop effective procedures and tools for implementation or intervention, so that the main elements of the model are remediable or applicable to others.
  8. Measuring the results obtained by applying the model.

Summary of steps in modeling - process

We can summarize the basic phases of the NLP modeling process in the following sequence:



  1. Define which experts and the contexts in which they use the ability to model, you want to model.
  2. Bestimme das entsprechende Verfahren zur Informationssammlung in den entsprechenden Kontexten - aus unterschiedlichen Wahrnehmungspositionen. Gehe von intuitiven Einsichten aus der zweiten Position aus, versuche dann, die Ergebnisse in Deiner eigenen ersten Position zu erreichen. Nehme eine dritte Position ein und achte darauf, wie sich Deine Art, ans Ziel zu kommen, von der der modellierten Person unterscheidet.
  3. Filter the results of information collection by relevant cognitive and behaviour patterns. Organize the patterns to a logical, coherent structure or to a "model".
  4. Test the effectiveness/usefulness of the model you have designed by testing it in different contexts and situations, and make sure you get the desired results.
  5. Reduce the model to the simplest and most elegant form that still produces the desired results.
  6. Find out the best methods to convey or "install" the explicit skills identified in the process of modeling.
  7. Specify which are the most suitable instruments to measure the results of the model and find out the limits or range for the validity of the model.

Robert Dilts Modelling Strategy


  1. Determine the desired skill or ability to be modelled, as well as the person(s) that possess this ability.
  2. Create a situation or context in which you will get at least three different specific examples from the people you want to model, as they show the desired skill.

    a) Find out the critical factors with the following perception filters in each example:

    • Access notices
    • Language patterns - Meta model, predicates, etc.
    • Physiology
    • Representation systems, strategies and submodalities
    • Meta Program Pattern
    • Faith sets and values
    • Logical levels
    b) Stelle fest, welche Faktoren in allen drei Beispielen die gleichen sind.
  3. Find at least one counter-example - i.e. another person or other person (including yourself) who is unable to adequately show the skill or situations in which the model could not adequately show the skill. Determine the critical factors of the counter-example by using the same filters as in step 2a.
  4. Compare the critical factors in the three successful examples to the critical factors in the counter-example(s). Pay attention to the significant differences.
  5. Change all significant critical factors of the counter-example / counter-examples, so that they correspond to the significant critical factors of the successful examples (match) until you get the desired behavior or the desired results from the individuals or situations that make up the counter-example / the counter-examples.
    If the change of these factors in the person/person or in the situation/situation does not lead to the desired behavior or result, then other more suitable or stronger examples for modeling and repeat the process from step 4 until the desired behavior or result is reached.
  6. Now start to change the critical factors that have contributed to achieving the desired behavior or result one by one.

    a) Find the border by establishing how far you can change the factor before it changes the result.

    b) Principle of elegance: Find the minimum number of factors that are necessary to achieve the desired behavior or results.

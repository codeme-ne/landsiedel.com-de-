What are strategies?

Strategies are the way we organize our thoughts and behavior to accomplish a task.

There are so-called macro- and micro-strategies. If, for example, someone sets himself the task of becoming a successful sociologist, the Macro-strategy the gradual development of this career: study, doctorate with summa cum laude. publications, employment at a prestigious university, habilitation, etc.

The Microstrategies These microstrategies can be analysed as certain processes within the sensory systems. They describe a specific internal way of processing sensory perceptions, i.e. also: Strategies are formal structures - specific procedures - which are initially completely independent of the respective content.

Each strategy also includes specific attitudes and Beliefs. In this case, for example, being successful is possible and important for me. I am talented and have the skills to such a career, etc.

Strategies are like the Recipe, with the help of which we bake a cake: Essential are the ingredients, decisive are the amount of the respective ingredient (whether an egg or ten) and the order in which we put it together. It makes a difference whether we add the egg before, during or after baking in the oven. i.e. the order of what we do within a strategy is just as important as what we do, even if everything happens within a few seconds. The ingredients of a strategy are the Representation systems, and the quantities and qualities are the Sub-modalitiesThe recipe is the big line of the strategy, the individual steps such as stirring the dough are the microunits of this strategy. The beliefs of successful baking would be, for example: baking a cake is possible/I can learn it and do it successfully/it's worth it, the cake will taste.

In the NLP, we investigate such strategies with the aim of finding out exactly what someone is doing if he is doing something successfully, so that he can make this ability accessible to others who have not yet had it available. Modelling.

We also, of course, analyse strategies that are less successful to find out how they can be improved.

There are different types of strategies, namely decision-making, motivation, learning, creativity, Relaxation and wake-up strategies, flirting strategy, partner choice strategy to name just a few.

The term strategies refers to mental processes in the NLP, which enable a person to translate his or her abilities into concrete behaviours in accordance with their beliefs and values. In the NLP, we assume that these processes are consciously and/or unconsciously through a series of sensory representations (VAKOGThese VAKOGs, which lead to a certain action, are usually partly purely internal, partly outward-looking processes.

Conditions of structural well-being of strategies

A. A strategy must have a sensoryly defined goal.

A strategy should have an information collection and feedback operation, with which a representation of the desired goal can be established and/or modified.

B. A strategy must include all three main presentation systems (V, A, K).

Each representation system (RS) has the ability to record and process information that the others do not have.

C. A strategy cannot reach the starting point without going through the decision point.

  1. No strategy without an EXIT point: A loop can occur if the surgical phase is so minimal that a significant change can occur by testing representations (comparison/contrast). However, this can also be done by an inadequate test.
  2. No two-point loop: such a loop arises when a person only commutes back and forth between two RSs.
  3. Ways to stop a strategy with an endless loop: Count or time! After the loop has made a certain number of loops or has run a certain time, the strategy must come to the decision point.

D. A strategy should have an external check after n steps

n depends on the type of activity. This check is necessary to obtain the external information you need when drawing, operating, playing the piano or the like.

Installing a Strategy

Installing a strategy means making a strategy (which it does not yet have in its repertoire) available to a person in such a way that this strategy can run "automatically" in the future. Possibilities include:


  1. the use of a chain anchor (in which the individual links of the "chain" correspond to individual steps of the new strategy),
  2. the conscious practice in the strategy sequence. Here, for example, a person is encouraged to change their body in accordance with the access instructions of the new strategy (e.g. to make exactly the right movements with the eyes),
  3. the interruption of existing, strongly ground-in strategies, e.g.

    • a) By overloading with (too) many (new) sensual impressions, or
    • b) by distraction (confusion)

Spelling strategy


  1. (Test) A person hears a word (auditive external stimulus),
  2. (Operate) constructed from it a visual image of the word,
  3. (Test) compares this image with the memory of the image of a word that it has ever read somewhere (visually constructed vs. visually reminiscent) - and (decision point) develops a feeling for the congruence or incongruence of these two images (kinesthetic).
  4. (further loop, further loops) If the test is negative [-], the process starts from the front (another visual image is constructed, ....). If the test is positive [+], the test becomes positive.
  5. (Exit) wrote down the word.

The optimal strategy for spelling the German language is a two-stage one: From visual memory to kinesthetic verification. Those persons who are very good at spelling see an inner image of the correctly written word and have a "feel" whether they wrote it correctly or incorrectly.

The procedure described below serves to install this spelling strategy. This approach is primarily intended for individual work with students with learning disabilities, who usually use an auditory - and thus inefficient - spelling strategy.

A. Preparation:

  • 1. Collect information about the visual access of the person concerned: Asking questions about visual memory, normally organized? (Eye movements)
  • Resources anchor:
    Finding positive experiences in which the person has successfully learned something difficult, e.g. cycling
    Ability to see and produce images: cinema screen, TV screen, fantasy, photos, joy, curiosity, etc.

B. Installing the V/K pattern

  • 1. Write words on paper strips and hold 30-40 cm in the pupil's ve position; students should now follow the letters with their eyes, memorize the word (fantasy: colored, with background,....); have snapshots made and remove paper strips
  • 2. Let the word be copied from the snapshot; let it be compared internally with the image in the image; "See if it feels right." (V/K); if not correct: 1. Repeat; if word looks right and feels right: anchor
  • 3. Further passages (as above) to internalize the V/K pattern let look at the word shorter and shorter, then let eyes in Ve position let word in the sentence context

C. Future bridging:

While the person imagines that he will learn new words or perform a spell test in the future, fire the anchor.

Decision-making strategy

Choosing from a menu is a good example of a decision strategy. Not everyone decides equally well! The quality of their own decision strategy has far-reaching consequences for our lives.

Typical problems with decision-making strategies

  1. Problems with producing alternatives:

    • Difficulties to construct anything at all; everything is remembered.
    • The number of alternatives is too small.
    • Just one way.
    • Just an either-or-alternative.
    • No stop while generating alternatives. No EXIT.
  2. Problems to represent the alternatives:

    • The alternatives are not in all RS This makes it difficult to assess the alternatives appropriately.
    • Inappropriate data. The person would have to go outside to get new and more data.
    • The alternatives are not adequately represented in the circumstances. In Sudan one should take a different inner picture of a meal than in a gourmet restaurant.
  3. Problems with evaluating alternatives:

    • The criteria for evaluating the alternatives are inappropriate, not adequate. For example, the choice of the therapist according to the brand of his shirts.
    • The criteria are not placed in an appropriate ranking, especially PRICE and other pre-selection criteria.
    • The different criteria are considered independently of each other. For example, not one alternative after another is evaluated with all criteria, but from one to the other and used with each other a different criterion. A polarity reaction is an example of this. Someone wants to buy cheaply at the end sale, but finds the goods unattractive and the ones he wants are too expensive for him. Two parts argue with each other.
    • The alternatives are not compared directly, point by point.

Redefinition of anchors

In essence, there is a Anchor any representation (internal or external) that triggers a different representation, a quadruple or a series of representations or quadruples (i.e. a strategy). Any part of an experience can be used as an anchor to trigger another part of the experience.

Anchors and access signals are the two most important tools in the implementation of strategies. We can use them to systematically trigger suitable representations in the right place within a strategy. Access signals can be imagined as self-built anchors.

Planning of context markers and decision points

Old results and the associated strategies, which have become inappropriate for a large part of situations, can continue to be effective in a few contexts. In such cases, the programmer installs a decision point in which any representation serves as a context marker. This should indicate in which situations which strategy is appropriate. If this action is omitted, then the ambiguity or overlap of the two contexts can cause a disturbance, which could trigger both strategies at the same time. The person does not know which strategy to apply. By reacting to both strategies, it is immobilized. Something tells you to do this, but something else looks more appropriate or feels better.

The representation, which serves as a mark, can assume any content. It could be a certain sound threshold, a certain word or class of words, a positive or negative kinesthetic Feeling or around an image or a distinction that is perceived in the environment. The purpose of the signal is to distinguish which context is suitable for which strategy.

Installing a Strategy

There are two basic ways of installing a strategy sequence that you have designed:


  1. Anchors strategy steps and their implementation.
  2. The client's Strategy sequence let it be practiced (form of the self anchor).

It is best to use both: While the person is "guided" by the strategy, fire the anchor you have set up before.

Purpose of the installation


  1. Each strategy step must automatically trigger the next one.
  2. The strategy sequence must be connected to the appropriate context - the sequence is bound to a stimulus (to a context marker) or anchored.

The effective installation of a strategy requires that you break or interrupt the existing strategy in the right place.

Installation with the help of anchors

There is a fundamental difference between the Anchors in the context of: Utilization procedure and anchoring in the context of the installation. When you use it, you anchor to control the content of certain strategy steps. When you install, you have to control the strategy step yourself. In this case, you will not become a specific content, but the act of using a particular device. Representation system for a certain step. You create the anchors in such a way that they provide access to the use of certain representation systems or a sequence of representation systems.

A. Anchoring an entire strategy sequence

If a Strategy sequence This can be anchored with a single anchor, so that it can be inserted as a whole in a new sequence that is currently being designed.

It can also be inserted into a situation in which it was not previously available as a resource; it is then bound to contextual stimuli that characterize this situation, thus enabling a different behavioral choice within this context. It is installed as a resource in situations where the client wants choices in its goal.

In such cases, the strategy is usually taken from a context in which it occurs naturally, and installed in a context in which it does not occur or has not occurred so far. In this operation, it is important that you separate the strategy sequence as such from the "starter" (from the triggering stimulus) that triggered this sequence beforehand in the context from which you take it out.

Step 1 shows that a certain external, visual Stimulus in context A the strategy sequence Aid .... Ke initiates in a natural way. This whole sequence is now anchored with the anchor. In step 2 the strategy unit is anchored into the context B, in which so far a certain external auditory Stimulus initiated a Ki - Vi loop. So the person is now the strategy from context A in context B accessible as a resource.

B. Anchoring individual and unconnected strategy steps

Individual representation steps can be taken out of strategy sequences that have no relationship with each other, anchored and then anchored together in a sequence, so that they form a new strategy.

The anchors with which the respective strategy step is triggered can be kinesthetic in nature (e.g. contacting different body parts). However, they can also be performed in other representational systems, e.g. as internal or external visual stimuli. Words are of course also anchors; their advantage is that they are to a certain extent culturally standardized. Thus, the programmer can also use verbal anchors to bring representations into a sequence (in colloquial terms, this would be called "instructions").

You can increase the effectiveness of your anchor procedures by pairing or combining multiple anchors for a particular strategy step. When installing a strategy sequence, anchors can be paired from different representation systems to increase their effectiveness.

Interrupting Strategies

Sometimes an existing strategy runs on beaten paths or is unusually heavily ground. If the result of this old strategy disturbs your attempts and if it causes behaviors that do not allow the achievement of metaresults, then it is the best thing for the client if you interrupt the existing strategy.

There are three basic ways of interrupting a strategy:


  1. By overloading
  2. by deflection; and
  3. by "Spinning" of the strategy

  • 1) An overload occurs when more information flows into a strategy or a strategy step than it can process. "Being overwhelmed by feelings, stunned by smell, etc.".

    The behavioral result of the overload is that a person's strategy cannot complete its cycle. If a strategy is completely interrupted, then the individual is presented without a next behavioral step and will start on the next behavioral step, which is offered to him from the situation. People in such a situation have a strong tendency, on each Anchor to respond to reactions that are offered to them out of the situation. You can use this phenomenon to install a new strategy by tuning your anchors correctly in time.
  • 2) A strategy is distracted when a certain input takes the representation sequence away from the current strategy. A person sunk in his mind is interrupted when a sound or movement draws his attention to the external environment. The stimulus does not overburden the strategy of the person, but rather takes over the current sequence and shows the behavior to another place.

    Strategies can be interrupted and distracted if you move your hands back and forth in front of a person and thereby their Eye positions A depressive person can be quickly and effectively removed from his negative state when he is asked to sit straight down, hold his head up, breathe fully in his chest.
  • 3) Interruption by "spinning" a strategy

    A strategy "spins itself out" when the end of the strategy is anchored with its beginning, so that the strategy always re-couples itself (like the snake in the saying that devours its tail). Because the strategy cannot come to an end, it is forced to continue in a loop. In most strategies a test is incorporated - one could call it a meta test, which can be converted into a completely new strategy, if the strategy operations prove to be ineffective after a certain time - for this purpose the "spinning" exists.

Error sources when installing a strategy

If a strategy does not work, check the following points:


  1. Calibration strategy (the chain is no stronger than its weakest link)

  2. Smooth transitions between strategy steps

  3. Review of congruence (clarification of personal history, assurance that no step anchors multiple reactions - context markers)

  4. Well-formedness

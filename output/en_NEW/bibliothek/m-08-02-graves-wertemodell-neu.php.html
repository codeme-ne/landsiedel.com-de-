Maslow's need pyramid

Abraham Maslow's need pyramid was in the 1930s the most famous model for the ontogenetic development of a human being. However, his theory was not founded and gave Clare Graves - a contemporary Maslows - cause for criticism.

In his opinion, Maslow's view of self-updated man was too inflexible. He did not accept the end point of the Maslow pyramid as the end point of development, because Graves believed that certain value systems (=memes) developed in response to certain human conditions of existence. Such memes are, on the one hand, psychic attempts to respond appropriately to certain historical conditions of existence and, on the other hand, these counter-movements to the respective dominant value system (antithetics).

Graves Levels

1. About Clare W. Graves

Clare W. Graves (1914-1986) was an American psychology professor at Union College in New York and with his meme model the pioneer of spiral dynamics. The impetus for the development of this model was given by his students in the central question: Why are people different? Why do some change? Why do some understand the highest human development goal of "self-realization" so completely different things?

Grave's answer was: "In short, I claim that the psychology of mature humans is a unfolding, oscillational, spiral-like process, characterized by the subordination of older behavioral systems with a lower rank, which progresses to newer higher systems above when the existential problems of humanity change."

By this he means that human development is continuously and never finished. It changes with the change of human conditions of existence and thus creates new systems into which the old systems are integrated. If a new system or a new stage develops, then we also change our psychology and our habits of life in order to adapt to these new conditions. The most diverse forms of life in this potentially open value system are possible. An end stage, which all must reach, does not exist. The individual, company or even whole societies, can only react positively to management principles, motivational incentives, education principles and allowed or ethical ideas, which correspond to the current level of this human existence!

Graves value model:

The emerging cyclic double helix model of mature, adult biopsychosocial behavior

Short description

Each class of problems or challenges is solved with the creation of a value system. Up to now, humanity has produced eight different value systems: Such value systems are mechanisms that the psyche has developed in order to be able to deal adequately with certain environmental situations. If the problems and challenges faced by a person or a society change, then their value system also changes. Therefore, value systems are appropriate if they enable a congruent problem-solution behavior. However, they cannot be compared independently of the context.

emerging = emerging
Value systems arise again and again by changing the milieu

Cyclic double helix

Each value system is created at the intersection of two forces, namely the problem and the respective problem-solving approach.

A system of values reflects how people think, i.e. their values and structures of faith. However, they must not be confused with the topic or content of thought. Rather, systems of values are a scheme or a way of thinking. Often, systems of values that are diametrically opposed in content, such as a fundamentalist religion and so-called "atheist communism", are merely forms of the same system of values.

The Graves model has an enormous explanatory and predictive value and is applicable to all aspects of biological, psychological and sociological behavior. It explains both individual behavior and international political interactions: Different thinking regarding religion, the meaning of human existence, economics, politics, family, education, health and leisure, as well as different patterns of goals, ethics, motivation, lifestyle and organization. Entering or leaving the respective system is triggered by forces from the milieu in which the respective system exists. However, the deep structures of the Graves model correspond to types of thinking, not types of people. These patterns represent biopsychosocial systems and are neither a typology of humans nor discrete levels of human development. They reflect systems in the repertoire of human behavioral possibilities, which is why each person has certain mental patterns that can be activated by different contexts and situations.

The Properties of the Layers

The emerging cyclic double helix model for the biopsychosocial development of adults postulates eight levels:

  • Beige: Reactive, autistic, archaic-instinctive; survival or survival struggle in the foreground
  • Violet Animistic, tribalistic, security-oriented. The person sacrifices his own wishes for the tribe, the chief or the spirits
  • Red self-centered, self-oriented, hedonistic, magical. The self wants to express itself here and now and does not care about others
  • Blue Absolutist, conformist, pious. The self sacrifices itself for later wages (in heaven, etc.)
  • Orange success-oriented, materialistic, objectiveistic. The self calculates one's own interests in order not to get into conflict with others
  • Green Personalistic, group-oriented, humanistic. The self is willing to sacrifice itself for others
  • Yellow Systemic, existential, functional. The self is interested in the current self-expression, but not at the expense of others.
  • Turquoise experience-oriented, global thinking, synergistic. The self is now ready to sacrifice itself so that life can continue.

The problems of the levels

Each level of the Graves model represents a solution strategy and a response to a class of problems. Therefore, each level can be seen as a link between a group of problems or challenges that people think up in order to cope with the corresponding problems.

The Memes in the model of Spiral Dynamics

Introduction

Clare Graves' students have expanded his model and created the spiral dynamics - one of the most precise models of cultural development. However, Spiral Dynamics has no one-dimensional perspective and does not look at people with a cold analytical distance, but provides a clear, deep insight into the flowing patterns of human psyche. Convictions and values often unconsciously guide our decisions or shape our identity.

The personal experiences are placed in an overall historical context of our human development, leading to liberating objectivity. This development is part of every human being and ranges from the most primitive survival instinct (beige) to spiritual aspirations (turquoise). 1. When resolving conflicts (quoted according to O.p.a.l.)

Because large companies usually find a mixture of the levels blue, orange and green, conflicts are preprogrammed.

Example:
The "Oranges" reject the "Blues" because they block changes from their point of view and they reject the "Greens" because they always want to integrate all opinions and therefore discuss endlessly. The "Greens" do not like the heartless, money-oriented "Oranges" and distrust the "Blues" with their hierarchies and established structures.

But let's fill the example with a little more life:

Human resources departments are often "green" and are committed to the well-being of employees (e.g. through personnel development, employee incentives). Management is often "orange". For them, the performance of employees is the first priority. For this reason, some managers consider personnel development a waste of time. The "greens" evaluate the "oranges" and the "oranges" do not understand the "greens".

Can you explain conflicts in your company?
What are your hypotheses?
The problem is that every person and every company considers their own level to be the only one right and attacks the others. At our current level, we also evaluate our old development levels, which we have already left behind us. We have developed further and now feel superior to people of the lower levels. We cannot yet understand the thinking of people of the upper stages of development.
Only from the level yellow (7 in the Graves model) we have an integral view. From this level we value people at all levels.

2. In the field of employee management

2.1 Problematik in Unternehmen

A person, a company or even an entire nation can only react positively to the management principles, motivational incentives, educational principles and ethical values that correspond to its own development level.
This means that the individual development stages require different leadership and communication styles as well as procedures. For example, "blue" employees can motivate you well with a title or status symbol. Meanwhile, "orange" employees are to be inspired with a performance-oriented award. These incentives would run ineffectively in the sand with "green" employees. Here, team work in a "valuable" project is more promising.

However, our level of development is not static, but is changing. Every person / system develops upwards in the long term with increasing complexity of the environment. If we have taken a new level of development, we change our view of the world, our way of thinking and living habits in order to adapt to the new conditions. We will then carry the stages of development within us in the future.

However, a person/system can fall back to a lower level in crises. For example, in recession and unemployment, social thinking (level green) can first go back and hard competition (level orange or red) can arise. If we have successfully mastered the crisis, we will quickly return to our "old" stage of development.

Values and rules of life

Values find their expression in terms such as security, love, honesty, honor, loyalty, reliability, responsibility, etc. They are behind our behaviors and are inner motivators. If you want to change your behavior, but have values that contradict the desired behavior, then you will not succeed. Your new behavior will not last long.

Only if your values support your behavior will you manage to change your life permanently. Because from our values we also shape our Life rules.

What are values?

Definition: Generally formulated as abstract nominalizations, values are of the highest importance for individuals as well as for companies and society. Values are the driving force and orientation for our behavior, and serve as criteria for the subsequent evaluation or evaluation of our actions. Values constitute an ideal frame of reference that guides our activities and assessments and directs us towards achieving the desired results. They must be experienced in a concrete interaction framework and be "negotiated" there, because they form the basis for our judgments about what makes life worth living.

Each person uses his/her personal values to move towards his/her goals and results. The feeling of personal satisfaction and totality results in a match between current behavior and personal values. The people you love or with whom you are friends, the way you raise your children, the political direction you support, how you carry out your work, the clothes you wear, the food you consume - all this is determined by the individual values you claim for yourself.

Species

Social values: Basic values on the basis of which a company is built (standards).
Examples: Security, Freedom, Justice, Equality and Progress

Ethical values: Everyone has their own ethical values according to which they act and evaluate their environment.
Examples: success, respect, honesty, health

With a view to the Motivational factors, you can divide them as follows:

  • Change: freedom (thought, action), stimulation (emotional, intellectual) etc.
  • Personal reinforcement: success, power, material, etc.
  • Strengthening social: care, justice, tolerance, love, etc.
  • Preservation: compliance with rules, tradition, nature conservation, health, etc.

Origin

In addition to ethics, values are also an important aspect in sociology, pedagogy, psychology and theology. Values are often not rational, but rather emotional, driven, aesthetic, moral or religious. Using values, we judge what is good/bad, pleasant/unpleasant, true/false, beautiful/ugly or appropriate/unfair.

Since values are very personal, strongly emotional orientation principles and motivators and include our beliefs, the same development periods apply for their development as for the appropriation of belief systems:
The time of "unconscious modelling" through introjection and identification with the immediate educators follows a time of extra-family socialization in school, the group of peers and the workplace; finally, values can also be integrated by conscious modelling.

Sources of the development of our values are: our family of origin, our friends, the community in which we grow up, social institutions (school, church, associations, etc.), the working environment and the economic, social and political conditions. Values are thus strongly linked to our identity and our beliefs and attitudes.

Values are the most unconscious parts of our personality. Values are principles, standards or qualities that are accepted as valuable or desirable by individuals or groups.

Values are closely linked to the beliefs, attitudes and meta-programs of the individual person. The terms: honor, responsibility, friendship, love, creativity, etc. can be values and criteria that serve a person as a pattern of orientation and sorting principles of the circumstances and activities in each context. Perceived facts and actions are sorted out by our five senses and personal activities are aimed at achieving the desired values, whereby different persons of a community, an institution, or a company can possess different values and criteria. Faith in certain things can also express itself in the form of values. Thus, faith in religion or a particular God can also be a value.

What are my values?

Values and criteria for the individual can be found by observing the nominalizations used or by simple questions such as:

  • "What inspired you to accept your current job?"
  • "What is the most important thing within your social relationships?"
  • "What do you consider to be particularly valuable in your life?"
  • "Why are you doing this?"

Individual work


  1. What projects, plans, projects, leisure activities have you carried out during the last 2-5 years? Create a list, find the associated values and enter them into a percentage breakdown.
  2. Which projects, plans, projects, leisure activities do you want to carry out during the next 2-5 years? Create a list and find out the corresponding values.

(Alternatively or in addition to 1 and 2, one could also ask: "How do you spend your time?" "What do you spend your money for?" "What do you invest energy for?". The more precisely these questions are answered, the more you know about the real preferences and thus indirectly about the acting values. What a person knows to say consciously about his values will always be a hopeless mixture of rationalization, idealized self-image and some true self-observations).

Example

The values could be, for example: passion, imagination, freedom, development, adventure, success and health.

Now I wonder what value is more important to me.

Is passion or imagination more important to me? Passion.

Is passion more important than freedom?

What is more important: passion or development? ...

So I compare all values in turn with passion. If there is no value that is more important than passion, then passion comes first. If there is a value that is more important, it is preferred and compared with all others. So if, for example, success is more important than passion, then success becomes number one. If you consistently go through this, you have very quickly brought your highest values into a row of ranks.

Difficulties in classification

At some points it may be difficult for you to classify.

Example: Is freedom more important to me or adventure? When I experience adventure, I feel free; without freedom I cannot experience adventure.

Suggestion: If you don't get along like this, ask yourself, "What does freedom and adventure mean to me?"

Freedom could mean doing and leaving what you want. Adventures could mean tackling exciting challenges. Now you can compare better: What is more important for me: doing and letting what I want to do or master exciting challenges? Exciting challenges - adventure is therefore a higher value. If you still cannot make a clear distinction despite inquiries, then ask yourself: "What would happen if one of the two values fell away." Could I imagine a life without adventure? It would be boring, I would be energyless and would have little incentive. How would it be if I had no freedom? I would be dependent. Is it worse to be dependent or energyless? Energyless. Thus adventure is more important than freedom. Would I prefer not to have freedom, but adventure? Or is it more pleasant to give up on the adventures and have freedom for it?

Change in my value hierarchy


  1. Identify a hierarchy of personal values
  2. Decision of changing the hierarchy of values: Find out with the interlocutor which of his personal values he wants to change in the position of the established hierarchy.
  3. Change of the value hierarchy: Find the Sub-modalities, Meta-programs, Strategies, Representation systems the most important value and the value to be changed.
    Use the sub-modalities of the most important value and transfer them to the value to be changed. Check whether meta programs, strategies etc. have already changed. If not, use the change of meta programs and strategies to support this process.
  4. Creating a new hierarchy of personal values: Let the communication partner reorder its hierarchy of personal values. How would you now arrange your personal values? Where is the changed value now?

Values and Life History Development


  1. Identify a hierarchy of personal values
    The communication partner is encouraged to name the personal values of his life: "What do you consider to be particularly valuable in your life?" Create with the partner a hierarchy of these personal values by using assessment criteria: "Which of these values is particularly important to you, less important and least important?
  2. Structure of the most important value and evidence
    Curse out the strategy or evidence for the most important value: "How do you know this is your most important value?" "How is this value represented in your thinking?"
  3. Evolution of value in life history
    Anchor the evidence (presentation, sounds, voice, etc.) and the associated sense of value and let all associated or associated experiences and experiences be remembered.
  4. Generalisation, belief system, meta-programs
    Let the life-historical examples of value look again and find out the structure:
    "What generalizations do you draw from the examples?
    "What is in the foreground in the process of perception –
    People, self, goals, activities etc.? (meta-programs)"
    "What do you think about the world from this perspective?
    "What do you think about yourself in these contexts?
  5. Future Pace
    Transfer the most important value to the future:
    "How will you shape your life if you keep this value to yourself and behave accordingly?"

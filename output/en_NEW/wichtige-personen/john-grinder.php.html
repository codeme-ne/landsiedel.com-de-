John Grinder -- Co-founder of Neurolinguistic Programming NLP

In addition to Richard Bandler, John Thomas Grinder is one of the two co-founders of Neuro-Linguistic Programming (NLP). As a linguist, he introduced a unique scientific perspective into the development and development of NLP. His research on transformational grammar, his work with Richard Bandler and his deep understanding of language and communication played a crucial role in the field of communication psychology of the 20th century.

Early years and training

About John Grinder's childhood is little publicly documented. He was born on Jan 10, 1939 in Michigan, USA. He studied philosophy at the University of San Francisco, where he then entered the military and served in Europe. After working for several years as an undercover agent of the CIA, he began his Linguistics studies at the University of California in San Diego at the end of the 1960s, which he completed with a doctorate. He specialized in the topic of transformation charting after Noam Chomsky. His interest in structural language, in particular the syntax and the construction of linguistic meaning, became evident early on.

Academic background

After completing his doctorate in linguistics, Grinder worked in the 1960s and 1970s as an assistant professor at the University of California in Santa Cruz at Kesge College under Gregory Bateson, who in some ways was a mentor to Grinder. There Grinder published scientific papers on the grammar structure of Spanish dialects and taught theoretical linguistics with a focus on generative grammar.

Meeting with Richard Bandler & Cooperation - The Joint Development of Neurolinguistic Programming

Grinder met at the beginning of the 70s the then psychology student Richard Bandler, who offered practical seminars on gestalt therapy at the UC Santa Cruz. Bandler asked Grinder to observe his work as supervisor/assistant professor of linguistics. Impressed by Bandler's intuitive understanding of linguistic mechanisms of action, Grinder began systematically researching his linguistic behaviour. Grinder describes her unusual activities and insight in detail in the work 'The Wild Days. NLP 1972-1981' together with Richard Bandler.

John Grinder and Richard Bandler combined their strengths: Bandler introduced the intuitive modeling of successful therapists, Grinder conducted an analysis of the linguistic patterns through his linguistic knowledge. Together they developed the meta-model of language, the typical linguistic distortions, generalizations and eradications identified. This model became the basis of their first joint book The Structure of Magic I (1975). Later they also examined nonverbal communication.

The meta-model was the starting point for further NLP techniques. Grinder and Bandler began to model outstanding communicators and therapists such as Milton Erickson, Virginia Satir and Fritz Perls. From this work a variety of new interventions and strategies emerged, such as the Milton model (pattern of hypnotic techniques), anchor techniques, reframing and submodality work.

Grinder always insisted on the exact description and transferability of the observed patterns, which gave NLP its methodological depth. In doing so, it was important to him that neurolinguistic programming is understood as a modelling discipline and not as a rigid set of methods.

NLP we are often used in coaching and therapy today.

Dissemination and teaching activities in the 1970s

After the development of the meta-model and the publication of The Structure of Magic, Grinder and Bandler began teaching NLP in seminars and workshops in the USA and later internationally. Demand for their content grew rapidly, especially after the publication of Patterns of the Hypnotic Techniques of Milton H. Erickson (1975/77), which documented their work with Erickson's language patterns.

Grinder was an active trainer and teacher during this time. Together with Bandler he led many of the early NLP trainings -- including the well-known training "Frogs into Princes", which appeared in 1979 as a book and made NLP known worldwide.

These years were characterized by intensive teaching activities, seminars for therapists, educators, business consultants and physicians. Grinder attached particular importance to the scientific foundation and structure of the model.

Many of today's well-known NLP trainers (e.g.Robert Dilts, Judith DeLozier, Steve Andreas, Leslie Cameron) also learned directly from Grinder and Bandler during this phase.

New projects and New Code NLP

In the 1980s Grinder increasingly distanced himself from the commercialization of the classical NLP. Together with Judith DeLozier and later with Carmen Bostic St. Clair, he developed the New Code of NLP. It focuses more on unconscious processes, holisticity, state work and ecology in the sense of sustainable change.

The New Code emphasizes that the coach or practitioner himself must be in an optimal condition to work effectively. Grinder developed formats such as the "Alphabet Game" and the "High Performance State Training".

John Grinder's training style and philosophy

John Grinder is clearly different in his style from Richard Bandler. He is more objective, structured, analytical. His trainings are highly experience-oriented, he uses many practical exercises, body language, feedback loops and attaches great importance to learning by doing. Grinder challenges his participants to take responsibility for their thinking and acting.

His philosophy is clear: NLP is not faith, but a toolbox. It is not about truths, but about what works. This pragmatic attitude runs through all his works.

Quotes and Anecdotes by John Grinder

John Grinder is known for his concise style, astute formulations and a pragmatic attitude. Here are some striking quotations:

"NLP is an attitude supported by a methodology that leaves behind a technique."

"If you can't model it, you don't understand it."

"There are no resist clients, only inflexible communicators."

Anecdotes:

In a training, Grinder provoked a participant who said he was "not creative enough" with more and more complex tasks. The learning effect arose when the participant stopped analyzing and acting simply. Grinder commented: "The body knows it long ago -- the mind is limping behind."

In Whispering in the Wind, he makes fun of NLPler, who avoid the meta model: "They develop a phobia in front of the meta model because it is too exhausting. Then they prefer to model their own avoidance -- that's at least consistent."

Influence and Legacy

John Grinder's influence on the field of personal development and communication is profound. He has not only played a major role in shaping the foundations of the NLP, but has also demonstrated new ways of making changes in a deeper, more sustainable and more ecological way.

His insistent attitude to precise modeling and his criticism of commercialization have helped many practitioners to look at NLP more differentiatedly. Many trainers and schools worldwide, including internationally renowned institutes, continue his methods or follow the New Code NLP.

Grinder's work has also intellectually enhanced NLP by its connection to linguistics and its claim to model human behavior systematically and transferably. His books are still regarded as basic works.

Up-to-date information

John Grinder is still active internationally today. Together with his wife Carmen Bostic St. Clair, he leads the International Trainers Academy of NLP, which is committed to quality assurance in NLP training.

He offers trainings and certifications worldwide -- especially on the New Code NLP -- and works closely with a network of qualified trainers. He is committed to training new generations of NLPs that use NLP with integrity, precision and ethical awareness.

In addition, he occasionally gives lectures at international conferences, writes articles and remains connected to the NLP community via digital channels. Despite his age, he remains a defining voice in the discourse on NLP, change work and personal excellence.

Important works by John Grinder

Grinder is the author and co-author of numerous influential books. A selection:

  • The Structure of Magic I (with Richard Bandler), Part II: A Book About Communication and Change -- Basics of NLP English: Metalanguage and Psychotherapy. The Structure of Magic Volume I & Communication and Change: Volume II
  • Frogs into Princes (with Richard Bandler) -- popular introduction to NLP
  • Reframing. An ecological approach to psychotherapy (NLP) - Introduction to Reframing (with Richard Bandler)
  • Patterns of the Hypnotic Techniques of Milton H. Erickson (with Richard Bandler)
  • Turtles All the Way Down (with Judith DeLozier) - Work with beliefs
  • Whispering in the Wind (with Carmen Bostic St. Clair) -- a comprehensive reflection on NLP and its origin
  • Therapy in Trance (concepts of human science) -- Neurolinguistic programming (NLP) and the structure of hypnotic communication - contains practice-oriented techniques and exercises on hypnosis with case studies from therapeutic contexts
